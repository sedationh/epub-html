When you do all this with radical open-mindedness, you will become more aware not just of what you’re seeing, but what you’re not seeing and what others, perhaps, are. DECIDE WELL
好好决定

Using decision-making logic to produce the best long-term outcomes has become its own science—one that employs probabilities and statistics, game theory, and other tools. While many of these tools are helpful, the fundamentals of effective decision making are relatively simple and timeless—in fact they are genetically encoded in our brains to varying degrees. Watch animals in the wild and you’ll see that they instinctively make expected value calculations to optimize the energy they expend to find food. Those that did this well prospered and passed on their genes through the process of natural selection; those that did it poorly perished. While most humans who do this badly won’t perish, they will certainly be penalized by the process of economic selection.
使用决策逻辑来产生最佳的长期结果已成为一门科学——它采用概率和统计、博弈论和其他工具。虽然这些工具中有许多是有帮助的，但有效决策的基本原理相对简单和永恒——事实上，它们在不同程度上在我们的大脑中进行了基因编码。观察野外的动物，您会发现它们会本能地进行期望值计算，以优化它们寻找食物所消耗的能量。那些做得很好的人繁荣起来，并通过自然选择过程传递了他们的基因;那些做得不好的人就死了。虽然大多数做得不好的人不会灭亡，但他们肯定会受到经济选择过程的惩罚。

As previously explained, there are two broad approaches to decision making: evidence/logic-based (which comes from the higher- level brain) and subconscious/emotion-based (which comes from the lower-level animal brain).
如前所述，有两种广泛的决策方法：基于证据/逻辑的（来自高级大脑）和基于潜意识/情感的（来自较低级别的动物大脑）。

5.5 Logic, reason, and common sense are your best tools for synthesizing reality and understanding what to do about it.
5.5 逻辑、理性和常识是你综合现实和理解如何应对它的最佳工具。

Be wary of relying on anything else. Unfortunately, numerous tests by psychologists show that the majority of people follow the lower-level path most of the time, which leads to inferior decisions without their realizing it. As Carl Jung put it, “Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate.” It’s even more important that decision making be evidence-based and logical when groups of people are working together. If it’s not, the process will inevitably be dominated by the most powerful rather than the most insightful participants, which is not only unfair but suboptimal. Successful organizations have cultures in which evidence-based decision making is the norm rather than the exception.
警惕依赖其他任何东西。不幸的是，心理学家的大量测试表明，大多数人大部分时间都遵循较低层次的道路，这导致了他们没有意识到的劣质决定。正如 Carl Jung 所说，“除非你让无意识变得有意识，否则它将指导你的生活，你会称之为命运。当一群人一起工作时，决策要基于证据和逻辑就显得尤为重要。否则，这个过程将不可避免地由最有权势的参与者主导，而不是最有洞察力的参与者，这不仅不公平，而且不是最佳选择。成功的组织拥有这样的文化，即基于证据的决策是常态而不是例外。

5.6 Make your decisions as expected value calculations.
5.6 按照预期价值计算做出决策。

Think of every decision as a bet with a probability and a reward for being right and a probability and a penalty for being wrong. Normally a winning decision is one with a positive expected value, meaning that the reward times its probability of occurring is greater than the penalty times its probability of occurring, with the best decision being the one with the highest expected value.
将每个决定都视为一个赌注，正确时有概率和奖励，错误时有概率和惩罚。通常，获胜的决策是具有正预期值的决策，这意味着奖励乘以其发生的概率大于惩罚乘以其发生的概率，最佳决策是具有最高期望值的决策。

Let’s say the reward for being right is $100 and its probability is 60 percent, while the penalty for being wrong is also $100. If you multiply the reward by the probability of being right you get $60 and if you multiply the penalty by the probability of being wrong (40 percent) you get $40. If you subtract the penalty from the reward, the difference is the expected value, which in this case is positive (+$20). Once you understand expected value, you also understand that it’s not always best to bet on what’s most probable. For example, suppose something that has only a one-in-five chance (20 percent) of succeeding will return ten times (e.g., $1,000) the amount that it will cost you if it fails ($100). Its expected value is positive ($120), so it’s probably a smart decision, even though the odds are against you, as long as you can also cover the loss. Play these probabilities over and over again and they will surely give you winning results over time.
假设正确的奖励是 100 美元，概率是 60%，而错误的惩罚也是 100 美元。如果你把奖励乘以正确的概率，你会得到 60 美元，如果你把惩罚乘以错误的概率 （40%），你会得到 40 美元。如果您从奖励中减去罚款，则差额为预期值，在本例中为正值 （+$20）。一旦你了解了预期价值，你也会明白，押注在最有可能的东西上并不总是最好的。例如，假设只有五分之一 （20%） 成功机会的东西将返回十倍（例如，1,000 美元）如果失败将花费的金额 （100 美元）。它的预期价值为正（120 美元），因此这可能是一个明智的决定，即使赔率对您不利，只要您也能弥补损失。一遍又一遍地玩这些概率，随着时间的推移，它们肯定会给你带来胜利的结果。

Though we mostly don’t carry out these calculations explicitly, we constantly make them intuitively. For example, when you decide to take an umbrella to the store even though there’s just a 40 percent chance of rain, or you check your phone to confirm the directions somewhere, even though you’re almost certain you know the way, you’re making expected value calculations.
虽然我们大多数情况下不会显式地执行这些计算，但我们总是凭直觉进行计算。例如，当您决定带伞去商店时，即使下雨的可能性只有 40%，或者您查看手机以确认某个地方的路线，即使您几乎确定自己知道路，您也在进行预期值计算。

Sometimes it’s smart to take a chance even when the odds are overwhelmingly against you if the cost of being wrong is negligible relative to the reward that comes with the slim chance of being right. As the saying goes, “It never hurts to ask.”
有时，即使赔率对你不利，如果犯错的代价相对于做对的机会渺茫所带来的回报可以忽略不计，那么冒险也是明智的。俗话说，“问从来没有坏处”。

This principle made a big difference in my own life. Years ago, when I was just starting my family, I saw a house that was perfect for us in every way. The problem was that it wasn’t on the market and everyone I asked told me the owner wasn’t interested in selling. To make matters worse, I was pretty sure I would be turned down for an adequate mortgage. But I figured that it wouldn’t cost me anything to call the owner to see if we could work something out. As it turned out, not only was he willing to sell, he was willing to give me a loan!
这个原则对我自己的生活产生了很大的影响。几年前，当我刚刚组建家庭时，我看到了一个在各个方面都非常适合我们的房子。问题是它没有在市场上，我问的每个人都告诉我，业主对出售不感兴趣。更糟糕的是，我很确定我会被拒绝获得足够的抵押贷款。但我想，打电话给业主，看看我们是否能解决一些问题，我不需要花任何钱。事实证明，他不仅愿意出售，还愿意给我贷款！

The same principle applies when the downside is terrible. For example, even if the probability of your having cancer is low, it might pay to get yourself tested when you have a symptom just to make sure.
当缺点很糟糕时，同样的原则也适用。例如，即使您患癌症的可能性很低，当您出现症状时，进行自我检测也可能是值得的。

To help you make expected value calculations well, remember that:
为了帮助您更好地进行预期值计算，请记住：

a. Raising the probability of being right is valuable no matter what your probability of being right already is. I often observe people making decisions if their odds of being right are greater than 50 percent. What they fail to see is how much better off they’d be if they raised their chances even more (you can almost always improve your odds of being right by doing things that will give you more information). The expected value gain from raising the probability of being right from 51 percent to 85 percent (i.e., by 34 percentage points) is seventeen times more than raising the odds of being right from 49 percent (which is probably wrong) to 51 percent (which is only a little more likely to be right). Think of the probability as a measure of how often you’re likely to be wrong. Raising the probability of being right by 34 percentage points means that a third of your bets will switch from losses to wins. That’s why it pays to stress-test your thinking, even when you’re pretty sure you’re right.
a. 无论您已经正确的可能性是多少，提高正确的概率都是有价值的。我经常观察到人们在做决定时，如果他们正确的几率大于 50%。他们没有看到的是，如果他们进一步提高机会，他们的生活会好得多（你几乎总是可以通过做一些能给你更多信息的事情来提高你做对的几率）。将正确的概率从 51% 提高到 85%（即 34 个百分点）的预期值增益是将正确的概率从 49%（这可能是错误的）提高到 51%（正确的可能性仅高一点）的 17 倍。将概率视为您可能出错的频率的度量。将正确的概率提高 34 个百分点意味着您有三分之一的赌注将从输变为赢。这就是为什么对你的思维进行压力测试是值得的，即使你非常确定你是对的。

b. Knowing when not to bet is as important as knowing what bets are probably worth making. You can significantly improve your track record if you only make the bets that you are most confident will pay off.
b. 知道何时不下注与知道哪些赌注可能值得下注一样重要。如果您只押您最有信心会得到回报的赌注，您可以显着改善您的业绩记录。

c. The best choices are the ones that have more pros than cons, not those that don’t have any cons at all. Watch out for people who argue against something whenever they can find something—anything—wrong with it, without properly weighing all the pluses and minuses. Such people tend to be poor decision makers.
c. 最好的选择是那些优点多于缺点的选择，而不是那些根本没有任何缺点的选择。当心那些只要能找到某件事——任何事情——问题就反对某件事的人，而没有适当地权衡所有的优点和缺点。这样的人往往是糟糕的决策者。

5.7 Prioritize by weighing the value of additional information against the cost of not deciding.
5.7 通过权衡额外信息的价值与不做决定的成本来确定优先级。

Some decisions are best made after acquiring more information; some are best made immediately. Just as you need to constantly sort the big from the small when you are synthesizing what’s going on, you need to constantly evaluate the marginal benefit of gathering more information against the marginal cost of waiting to decide. People who prioritize well understand the following:
有些决定最好在获得更多信息后做出;有些最好立即制作。正如您在综合正在发生的事情时需要不断地区分大与小一样，您需要不断评估收集更多信息的边际收益与等待决定的边际成本。确定优先级的人会理解以下内容：

a. All of your “must-dos” must be above the bar before you do your “like-to-dos.” Separate your “must-dos” from your “like-to-dos” and don’t mistakenly slip any “like-to-dos” onto the first list.
a. 在你做你的 “喜欢做的事情 ”之前，你所有的 “必做 ”都必须在标准以上。将您的 “必做 ”与 “喜欢的事情 ”分开，不要错误地将任何 “喜欢的事情 ”放在第一个列表中。

b. Chances are you won’t have time to deal with the unimportant things, which is better than not having time to deal with the impor-tant things. I often hear people say, “Wouldn’t it be good to do this or that?” It’s likely they are being distracted from far more important things that need to be done well.
b. 你很可能没有时间处理不重要的事情，这比没有时间处理重要的事情要好。我经常听到人们说，“做这个或那个不是很好吗？他们很可能被分散了注意力，而忽略了需要做好的更重要的事情。

c. Don’t mistake possibilities for probabilities. Anything is possible. It’s the probabilities that matter. Everything must be weighed in terms of its likelihood and prioritized. People who can accurately sort probabilities from possibilities are generally strong at “practical thinking”; they’re the opposite of the “philosopher” types who tend to get lost in clouds of possibilities.
c. 不要将可能性误认为是概率。一切皆有可能。重要的是概率 一切都必须根据其可能性进行权衡并确定优先级。能够准确地将概率与可能性区分开来的人通常很擅长“实践思维”;他们与倾向于迷失在可能性云雾中的“哲学家”类型相反。

SHORTCUTS FOR BECOMING A GREAT DECISION MAKER
成为优秀决策者的捷径

Great decision makers don’t remember all of these steps in a rote way and carry them out mechanically, yet they do follow them. That’s because through time and experience they’ve learned to do most of them reflexively, just as a baseball player catches a fly ball without thinking about how he’s going to do it. If they had to call each of the principles up from their memory and then run them through their slow conscious minds, they couldn’t possibly handle all the things that are coming at them well. But there are a couple of things that they do carry out consciously and you should do them too.
伟大的决策者不会死记硬背所有这些步骤并机械地执行它们，但他们确实会遵循它们。那是因为随着时间的推移和经验，他们已经学会了条件反射地完成其中的大部分，就像棒球运动员接住飞球而不考虑他将如何接球一样。如果他们必须从记忆中调用每一个原则，然后在他们缓慢的意识中运行它们，他们就不可能很好地处理所有向他们袭来的事情。但是有几件事他们确实有意识地进行，你也应该这样做。

5.8 Simplify!
5.8 简化！

Get rid of irrelevant details so that the essential things and the relationships between them stand out. As the saying goes, “Any damn fool can make it complex. It takes a genius to make it simple.” Think of Picasso. He could paint beautiful representational paintings from an early age, but he continually pared down and simplified as his career progressed. Not everyone has a mind that works that way, but just because you can’t do something naturally doesn’t mean you can’t do it—you just have to have creativity and determination. If necessary, you can seek the help of others.
去掉不相关的细节，这样重要的事情和它们之间的关系就会突出出来。俗话说，“任何该死的傻瓜都能让它变得复杂。让它变得简单需要一个天才。想想毕加索。他从小就能画出美丽的具象画，但随着事业的发展，他不断精简和简化。不是每个人都有这样运作的头脑，但仅仅因为你自然而然地不能做某事并不意味着你不能做它——你只需要有创造力和决心。如有必要，您可以寻求他人的帮助。

5.9 Use principles.
5.9 使用原则。

Using principles is a way of both simplifying and improving your decision making. While it might seem obvious to you by now, it’s worth repeating that realizing that almost all “cases at hand” are just “another one of those,” identifying which “one of those” it is, and then applying well-thought-out principles for dealing with it. This will allow you to massively reduce the number of decisions you have to make (I estimate by a factor of something like 100,000) and will lead you to make much better ones. The key to doing this well is to:
使用原则是简化和改进决策的一种方式。虽然现在对您来说似乎很明显，但值得重复的是，要意识到几乎所有的 “手头的案例” 都只是 “另一个”，确定它是哪个 “那些案例中的一个”，然后应用经过深思熟虑的原则来处理它。这将使你能够大大减少你必须做出的决定数量（我估计大约有 100,000 个），并引导你做出更好的决定。做好这一点的关键是：

1. Slow down your thinking so you can note the criteria you are using to make your decision.
1. 放慢你的思考速度，这样你就可以记下你用来做决定的标准。

2. Write the criteria down as a principle.
2. 将标准写下来作为原则。

3. Think about those criteria when you have an outcome to assess, and refine them before the next “one of those” comes along.
3. 当你有一个结果要评估时，考虑这些标准，并在下一个 “其中之一 ”出现之前完善它们。

Identifying which “one of those” each thing is is like identifying which species an animal is. Doing that for each thing and then matching it up with the appropriate principles will become like playing a game, so it will be fun as well as helpful. Of course it can also be challenging. Many “cases at hand,” as I call them, are hybrids. When a case at hand contains a few “another ones of those,” one must weigh different principles against each other, using mental maps of how the different types of things I encounter should be handled. To help people do that, I created a tool called a Coach, which is explained in the Appendix.
识别每个事物是哪一个“，就像识别动物是哪个物种一样。对每件事都这样做，然后将其与适当的原则相匹配，这将变得就像玩游戏一样，因此既有趣又有帮助。当然，它也可能具有挑战性。我称之为许多“手头的案件”都是混合体。当手头的案件包含一些“另一个案例”时，人们必须权衡不同的原则，使用心理地图来衡量我遇到的不同类型的事情应该如何处理。为了帮助人们做到这一点，我创建了一个名为 Coach 的工具，附录中对此进行了解释。

You can use your own principles, or you can use others’; you just want to use the best ones possible well. If you think that way constantly, you will become an excellent principled thinker.
你可以用你自己的原则，也可以用别人的原则;你只想用最好的。如果你一直这样想，你就会成为一个优秀的有原则的思考者。

5.10 Believability weight your decision making
5.10 可信度权重

I have found triangulating with highly believable people who are willing to have thoughtful disagreements has never failed to enhance my learning and sharpen the quality of my decision making. It typically leads me to make better decisions than I could have otherwise and it typically provides me with thrilling learning. I urge you to do it.
我发现与愿意有深思熟虑的分歧的高度可信的人进行三角测量从未失败过，这促进了我的学习并提高我的决策质量。它通常会引导我做出比我本来可以做出的更好的决定，并且它通常会为我提供令人兴奋的学习。我敦促你这样做。

To do it well, be sure to avoid the common perils of: 1) valuing your own believability more than is logical and 2) not distinguishing between who is more or less credible.
要做到这一点，一定要避免常见的危险：1） 重视你自己的可信度而不是逻辑，以及 2） 不区分谁更可信或更不可信。

In case of a disagreement with others, start by seeing if you can agree on the principles that should be used to make that decision. This discussion should include exploring the merits of the reasoning behind the different principles. If you agree on them, apply them to the case at hand and you’ll arrive at a conclusion everyone agrees on. If you disagree on the principles, try to work through your disagreement based on your respective believabilities. I will explain how we do this in more detail in Work Principles.
如果与他人有分歧，首先看看您是否能就应该用于做出该决定的原则达成一致。这种讨论应该包括探索不同原则背后的推理的优点。如果你同意它们，将它们应用到手头的案例中，你就会得出每个人都同意的结论。如果你不同意这些原则，试着根据你各自的可信度来解决你的分歧。我将在 工作原则 中更详细地解释我们如何做到这一点。

This sort of principled and believability-weighted decision making is fascinating and leads to much different and much better decision making than is typical. For example, imagine if we used this approach to choose the president. It would be fascinating to see which principles we would come up with both for determining what makes a good president as well as for deciding who is most believable in making such determinations. Would we wind up with something like one person one vote, or something different? And if different, in what ways? It certainly would lead to very different outcomes. During the next election, let’s do this in parallel with our ordinary electoral process so we can see the difference.
这种有原则和可信度加权的决策很吸引人，并导致与典型决策截然不同且更好的决策。例如，想象一下，如果我们使用这种方法来选择总统。看看我们会提出哪些原则来确定什么是好总统，以及决定谁最可信地做出这样的决定，这将是一件令人着迷的事情。我们最终会得到一人一票之类的东西，还是不同的东西？如果不同，在哪些方面？这肯定会带来非常不同的结果。在下一次选举期间，让我们与正常的选举过程同时进行，以便我们可以看到差异。

While believability-weighted decision making can sound complicated, chances are you do it all the time—pretty much whenever you ask yourself, “Who should I listen to?” But it’s almost certainly true that you’d do it a lot better if you gave more thought to it.
虽然可信度加权决策听起来很复杂，但您很可能一直在这样做——几乎每当您问自己“我应该听谁的话”时。但几乎可以肯定的是，如果你多考虑一下，你会做得更好。

5.11 Convert your principles into algorithms and have the computer make decisions alongside you.
5.11 将您的原则转化为算法，让计算机与您一起做出决策。

If you can do that, you will take the power of your decision making to a whole other level. In many cases, you will be able to test how that principle would have worked in the past or in various situations that will help you refine it, and in all cases, it will allow you to compound your understanding to a degree that would otherwise be impossible. It will also take emotion out of the equation. Algorithms work just like words in describing what you would like to have done, but they are written in a language that the computer can understand. If you don’t know how to speak this language, you should either learn it or have someone close to you who can translate for you. Your children and their peers must learn to speak this language because it will soon be as important or more important than any other language.
如果你能做到这一点，你的决策权就会提升到一个全新的水平。在许多情况下，你将能够测试这个原则在过去是如何运作的，或者在各种情况下会帮助你完善它，在所有情况下，它将允许你把你的理解复杂化到一个原本不可能的程度。它还将消除情感。算法的工作原理就像文字一样描述您想要做的事情，但它们是用计算机可以理解的语言编写的。如果您不知道如何说这种语言，您应该学习它或让您身边的人可以为您翻译。您的孩子和他们的同龄人必须学会说这种语言，因为它很快就会变得与任何其他语言一样重要或更重要。

By developing a partnership with your computer alter ego in which you teach each other and each do what you do best, you will be much more powerful than if you went about your decision making alone. The computer will also be your link to great collective decision making, which is far more powerful than individual decision making, and will almost certainly advance the evolution of our species.
通过与您的计算机另一个自我建立伙伴关系，在这种伙伴关系中，你们互相教导，每个人都做你最擅长的事情，你会比你一个人做决定更强大。计算机也将成为您与伟大集体决策的纽带，它比个人决策强大得多，并且几乎肯定会推动我们物种的进化。

SYSTEMIZED AND COMPUTERIZED DECISION MAKING
系统化和计算机化的决策

In the future, artificial intelligence will have a profound impact on how we make decisions in every aspect of our lives—especially when combined with the new era of radical transparency about people that’s already upon us. Right now, whether you like it or not, it is easy for anyone to access your digital data to learn a tremendous amount about what you’re like, and this data can be fed into computers that do everything from predict what you’re likely to buy to what you value in life. While this sounds scary to many people, at Bridgewater we have been combining radical transparency with algorithmic decision making for more than thirty years and have found that it produces remarkable results. In fact, I believe that it won’t be long before this kind of computerized decision making guides us nearly as much as our brains do now.
未来，人工智能将对我们生活的各个方面做出决策的方式产生深远影响，尤其是当与已经存在的对人彻底透明的新时代相结合时。现在，无论你喜欢与否，任何人都可以轻松访问你的数字数据，以大量了解你的情况，这些数据可以被输入到计算机中，这些计算机可以做任何事情，从预测你可能会买什么到你在生活中看重什么。虽然这对许多人来说听起来很可怕，但在 Bridgewater，我们三十多年来一直将彻底的透明度与算法决策相结合，并发现它产生了显着的结果。事实上，我相信用不了多久，这种计算机化的决策就会像我们现在的大脑一样指导我们。

The concept of artificial intelligence is not new. Even back in the 1970s, when I first started experimenting with computerized decision making, it had already been around for nearly twenty years (the term “artificial intelligence” was first introduced in 1956 at a conference at Dartmouth College). While a lot has changed since then, the basic concepts remain the same.
人工智能的概念并不新鲜。甚至在 1970 年代，当我第一次开始尝试计算机化决策时，它已经存在了近 20 年（“人工智能”一词于 1956 年在达特茅斯学院的一次会议上首次提出）。虽然从那时起发生了很多变化，但基本概念保持不变。

To give you just one ultrasimple example of how computerized decision making works, let’s say you have two principles for heating your home: You want to turn the heat on when the temperature falls below 68 and you want to turn the heat off between midnight and 5:00 a.m. You can express the relationship between these criteria in a simple decision-making formula: If the temperature is less than 68 degrees and the time is not between 5:00 a.m. and midnight, then turn on the heat. By gathering many such formulas, it’s possible to create a decision-making system that takes in data, applies and weighs the relevant criteria, and recommends a decision.
仅举一个关于计算机化决策如何运作的超简单示例，假设您有两个为家供暖的原则：您想在温度低于 68 度时打开暖气，并且想在午夜到凌晨 5：00 之间关闭暖气。您可以用一个简单的决策公式来表达这些标准之间的关系：如果温度低于 68 度并且时间不在凌晨 5：00 到午夜之间，则打开暖气。通过收集许多这样的公式，可以创建一个决策系统，该系统接收数据、应用和权衡相关标准，并推荐决策。

Specifying our investment decision-making criteria in algorithms and running historical data through them, or specifying our work principles in algorithms and using them to aid in management decision making, are just bigger and more complicated versions of that smart thermostat. They allow us to make more informed and less emotional decisions much faster than we could on our own.
在算法中指定我们的投资决策标准并通过它们运行历史数据，或者在算法中指定我们的工作原则并使用它们来帮助管理决策，只是智能恒温器的更大、更复杂的版本。它们使我们能够比自己更快地做出更明智、更少情绪化的决定。

I believe that people will increasingly do this and that computer coding will become as essential as writing. In time, we will use machine assistants as much for decision making as we do for information gathering today. As these machines help us, they will learn about what we are like—what we value, what our strengths and weaknesses are—and they will be able to tailor the advice they give us by automatically seeking out the help of others who are strong where we are weak. It won’t be long before our machine assistants are speaking to others’ machine assistants and collaborating in this way. In fact, that’s beginning to happen already.
我相信人们会越来越多地这样做，计算机编码将变得与写作一样重要。随着时间的推移，我们将像今天一样使用机器助手进行决策。当这些机器帮助我们时，它们将了解我们是什么样的——我们重视什么，我们的长处和短处是什么——并且它们将能够通过在我们弱小的地方自动寻求其他强者的帮助来定制它们给我们的建议。用不了多久，我们的机器助手就会与其他机器助手交谈并以这种方式进行协作。事实上，这已经开始发生。

Imagine a world in which you can use technology to connect to a system in which you can input the issue you’re dealing with and have exchanges about what you should do and why with the highest-rated thinkers in the world. We’ll soon be able to do this. Before too long, you will be able to tap the highest-quality thinking on nearly every issue you face and get the guidance of a computerized system that weighs different points of view. For example, you will be able to ask what lifestyle or career you should choose given what you’re like, or how to best interact with specific people based on what they’re like. These innovations will help people get out of their own heads and unlock an incredibly powerful form of collective thinking. We are doing this now and have found it way better than traditional thinking.
想象一下这样一个世界：在这个世界里，你可以使用技术连接到一个系统，在这个系统中，你可以输入你正在处理的问题，并与世界上评价最高的思想家交流你应该做什么以及为什么。我们很快就能做到这一点。用不了多久，您将能够就您面临的几乎所有问题利用最高质量的思考，并获得权衡不同观点的计算机化系统的指导。例如，您将能够询问鉴于您的情况应该选择什么样的生活方式或职业，或者如何根据他们的情况最好地与特定的人互动。这些创新将帮助人们走出自己的头脑，解锁一种非常强大的集体思维形式。我们现在正在这样做，并且发现它比传统思维要好得多。

While this kind of view often leads to talk of artificial intelligence competing with human intelligence, in my opinion human and artificial intelligence are far more likely to work together because that will produce the best results. It’ll be decades—and maybe never—before the computer can replicate many of the things that the brain can do in terms of imagination, synthesis, and creativity. That’s because the brain comes genetically programmed with millions of years of abilities honed through evolution. The “science” of decision making that underlies many computer systems remains much less valuable than the “art.” People still make the most important decisions better than computers do. To see this, you need look no further than at the kinds of people who are uniquely successful. Software developers, mathematicians, and game-theory modelers aren’t running away with all the rewards; it is the people who have the most common sense, imagination, and determination.
虽然这种观点经常导致人工智能与人类智能竞争的讨论，但在我看来，人类和人工智能更有可能协同工作，因为这将产生最好的结果。计算机需要几十年——也许永远不会——才能复制大脑在想象力、综合和创造力方面可以做的许多事情。那是因为大脑的基因编程具有数百万年通过进化磨练的能力。作为许多计算机系统基础的决策“科学”仍然远不如“艺术”有价值。人们仍然比计算机更好地做出最重要的决定。要看到这一点，你只需要看看那些独特的成功者就知道了。软件开发人员、数学家和博弈论建模者并没有逃避所有的回报;是那些拥有最常识、想象力和决心的人。

Only human intelligence can apply the interpretations that are required to provide computer models with appropriate input. For example, a computer can’t tell you how to weigh the value of the time you spend with your loved ones against the time you spend at work or the optimal mix of hours that will provide you with the best marginal utilities for each activity. Only you know what you value most, who you want to share your life with, what kind of environment you want to be in, and ultimately how to make the best choices to bring those things about. What’s more, so much of our thinking comes from the subconscious in ways we don’t understand, that thinking we can model it fully is as unlikely as an animal that has never experienced abstract thinking attempting to define and replicate it.
只有人类智能才能应用为计算机模型提供适当输入所需的解释。例如，计算机无法告诉您如何权衡与亲人共度的时间与工作时间的价值，或者为每项活动提供最佳边际效用的最佳时间组合。只有你知道你最看重什么，你想与谁分享你的生活，你想处于什么样的环境中，以及最终如何做出最佳选择来实现这些事情。更重要的是，我们的很多思考都来自潜意识，以我们无法理解的方式，以至于我们可以完全建模的思考就像从未经历过抽象思维的动物试图定义和复制它一样不可能。

Yet at the same time, the brain cannot compete with the computer in many ways. Computers have much greater “determination” than any person, as they will work 24/7 for you. They can process vastly more information, and they can do it much faster, more reliably, and more objectively than you could ever hope to. They can bring millions of possibilities that you never thought of to your attention. Perhaps most important of all, they are immune to the biases and consensus-driven thinking of crowds; they don’t care if what they see is unpopular, and they never panic. During those terrible days after 9/11, when the whole country was being whipsawed by emotion, or the weeks between September 19 and October 10, 2008, when the Dow fell 3,600 points, there were times I felt like hugging our computers. They kept their cool no matter what.
但与此同时，大脑无法在许多方面与计算机竞争。计算机比任何人都具有更大的“决心”，因为它们将为您 24/7 全天候工作。他们可以处理更多的信息，而且他们可以比您希望的更快、更可靠、更客观地处理这些信息。它们可以带来数百万种您从未想过的可能性。也许最重要的是，他们不受人群的偏见和共识驱动的思维的影响;他们不在乎他们看到的东西是否不受欢迎，他们从不惊慌。在 9/11 之后的那些可怕的日子里，当整个国家都被情绪鞭打时，或者在 2008 年 9 月 19 日至 10 月 10 日之间的几周里，当道琼斯指数下跌 3,600 点时，有时我想拥抱我们的电脑。无论如何，他们都保持冷静。

This combination of man and machine is wonderful. The process of man’s mind working with technology is what elevates us—it’s what has taken us from an economy where most people dig in the dirt to today’s Information Age. It’s for that reason that people who have common sense, imagination, and determination, who know what they value and what they want, and who also use computers, math, and game theory, are the best decision makers there are. At Bridgewater, we use our systems much as a driver uses a GPS in a car: not to substitute for our navigational abilities but to supplement them.
这种人与机器的结合真是太棒了。人类的大脑与技术合作的过程使我们得到了提升——它使我们从大多数人都在泥土中挖掘的经济时代发展到今天的信息时代。正是出于这个原因，具有常识、想象力和决心的人，知道自己看重什么和想要什么，并且还使用计算机、数学和博弈论的人，是最好的决策者。在布里奇沃特，我们使用我们的系统就像驾驶员在汽车中使用 GPS 一样：不是为了替代我们的导航能力，而是为了补充它们。

5.12 Be cautious about trusting AI without having deep understanding.
5.12 在没有深入理解的情况下谨慎信任 AI。

I worry about the dangers of AI in cases where users accept—or, worse, act upon—the cause-effect relationships presumed in algorithms produced by machine learning without understanding them deeply.
我担心 AI 的危险性，即用户接受（或者更糟糕的是，采取行动）机器学习生成的算法中假定的因果关系，而没有深入理解它们。

Before I explain why, I want to clarify my terms. “Artificial intelligence” and “machine learning” are words that are thrown around casually and often used as synonyms, even though they are quite different. I categorize what is going on in the world of computer-aided decision making under three broad types: expert systems, mimicking, and data mining (these categories are mine and not the ones in common use in the technology world).
在我解释原因之前，我想澄清一下我的术语。“人工智能”和“机器学习”是随意出现的词，经常用作同义词，尽管它们完全不同。我将计算机辅助决策领域中发生的事情分为三大类：专家系统、模仿和数据挖掘（这些类别是我的类别，而不是技术世界中常用的类别）。

Expert systems are what we use at Bridgewater, where designers specify criteria based on their logical understandings of a set of cause-effect relationships, and then see how different scenarios would emerge under different circumstances.
Bridgewater 使用专家系统，设计师根据他们对一组因果关系的逻辑理解来指定标准，然后看到在不同情况下如何出现不同的场景。

But computers can also observe patterns and apply them in their decision making without having any understanding of the logic behind them. I call such an approach “mimicking.” This can be effective when the same things happen reliably over and over again and are not subject to change, such as in a game bounded by hard-and-fast rules. But in the real world things do change, so a system can easily fall out of sync with reality.
但计算机也可以观察模式并将其应用于决策，而无需了解其背后的逻辑。我把这种方法称为 “模仿”。当相同的事情一遍又一遍地可靠地发生并且不受更改时，例如在受硬性规定约束的游戏中，这可能是有效的。但在现实世界中，事情确实会发生变化，因此系统很容易与现实不同步。

The main thrust of machine learning in recent years has gone in the direction of data mining, in which powerful computers ingest massive amounts of data and look for patterns. While this approach is popular, it’s risky in cases when the future might be different from the past. Investment systems built on machine learning that is not accompanied by deep understanding are dangerous because when some decision rule is widely believed, it becomes widely used, which affects the price. In other words, the value of a widely known insight disappears over time. Without deep understanding, you won’t know if what happened in the past is genuinely of value and, even if it was, you will not be able to know whether or not its value has disappeared—or worse. It’s common for some decision rules to become so popular that they push the price far enough that it becomes smarter to do the opposite.
近年来，机器学习的主要推动力已经朝着数据挖掘的方向发展，在这个方向上，强大的计算机会摄取大量数据并寻找模式。虽然这种方法很受欢迎，但在未来可能与过去不同的情况下，它存在风险。建立在机器学习之上的投资系统如果没有深入的理解是危险的，因为当某些决策规则被广泛相信时，它就会被广泛使用，从而影响价格。换句话说，广为人知的见解的价值会随着时间的推移而消失。如果没有深入的理解，你将无法知道过去发生的事情是否真的有价值，即使有，你也无法知道它的价值是否已经消失——或者更糟。一些决策规则变得如此流行，以至于它们将价格推得足够高，以至于反其道而行之变得更聪明，这是很常见的。

Remember that computers have no common sense. For example, a computer could easily misconstrue the fact that people wake up in the morning and then eat breakfast to indicate that waking up makes people hungry. I’d rather have fewer bets (ideally uncorrelated ones) in which I am highly confident than more bets I’m less confident in, and would consider it intolerable if I couldn’t argue the logic behind any of my decisions. A lot of people vest their blind faith in machine learning because they find it much easier than developing deep understanding. For me, that deep understanding is essential, especially for what I do.
请记住，计算机没有常识。例如，计算机很容易误解人们早上醒来然后吃早餐的事实，以表明醒来会让人感到饥饿。我宁愿少一些我高度有信心的赌注（最好是不相关的赌注），也不愿多一些我不太有信心的赌注，如果我不能争论我任何决定背后的逻辑，我会认为这是无法容忍的。很多人盲目地相信机器学习，因为他们发现这比深入理解要容易得多。对我来说，这种深刻的理解是必不可少的，尤其是对于我的工作。

I don’t mean to imply that these mimicking or data-mining systems, as I call them, are useless. In fact, I believe that they can be extremely useful in making decisions in which the future range and configuration of events are the same as they’ve been in the past. Given enough computing power, all possible variables can be taken into consideration. For example, by analyzing data about the moves that great chess players have made under certain circumstances, or the procedures great surgeons have used during certain types of operations, valuable programs can be created for chess playing or surgery. Back in 1997, the computer program Deep Blue beat Garry Kasparov, the world’s highest-ranked chess player, using just this approach. But this approach fails in cases where the future is different from the past and you don’t know the cause-effect relationships well enough to recognize them all. Understanding these relationships as I do has saved me from making mistakes when others did, most obviously in the 2008 financial crisis. Nearly everyone else assumed that the future would be similar to the past. Focusing strictly on the logical cause-effect relationships was what allowed us to see what was really going on.
我并不是说这些模仿或数据挖掘系统，正如我所说的，是无用的。事实上，我相信它们在做出事件的未来范围和配置与过去相同的决策时非常有用。如果有足够的计算能力，可以考虑所有可能的变量。例如，通过分析有关伟大国际象棋选手在某些情况下所采取的动作的数据，或伟大的外科医生在某些类型的手术中使用的程序，可以为国际象棋下棋或手术创建有价值的程序。早在 1997 年，计算机程序 Deep Blue 就使用这种方法击败了世界上排名最高的国际象棋选手 Garry Kasparov。但是，如果未来与过去不同，并且您对因果关系的了解不够，无法识别它们，则这种方法会失败。像我一样理解这些关系，使我免于犯别人犯的错误，尤其是在 2008 年金融危机中。几乎所有其他人都认为未来会与过去相似。严格关注逻辑因果关系使我们能够看到真正发生的事情。

When you get down to it, our brains are essentially computers that are programmed in certain ways, take in data, and spit out instructions. We can program the logic in both the computer that is our mind and the computer that is our tool so that they can work together and even double-check each other. Doing that is fabulous.
当你深入探讨这个问题时，我们的大脑本质上是以某种方式编程、接收数据并吐出指令的计算机。我们可以在作为我们思想的计算机和作为我们工具的计算机中对逻辑进行编程，以便它们可以一起工作，甚至相互仔细检查。这样做真是太棒了。

For example, suppose we were trying to derive the universal laws that explain species change over time. Theoretically, with enough processing power and time, this should be possible. We would need to make sense of the formulas the computer produces, of course, to make sure that they are not data-mined gibberish, by which I mean based on correlations that are not causal in any way. We would do this by constantly simplifying these rules until their elegance is unmistakable.
例如，假设我们试图推导出解释物种随时间变化的普遍定律。从理论上讲，如果有足够的处理能力和时间，这应该是可能的。当然，我们需要理解计算机生成的公式，以确保它们不是数据挖掘的胡言乱语，我的意思是基于任何都不是因果关系的相关性。我们将通过不断简化这些规则来做到这一点，直到它们的优雅明确无误。

Of course, given our brain’s limited capacity and processing speed, it could take us forever to achieve a rich understanding of all the variables that go into evolution. Is all the simplifying and understanding that we employ in our expert systems truly required? Maybe not. There is certainly a risk that changes not in the tested data might still occur. But one might argue that if our data-mining-based formulas seem able to account for the evolution of all species through all time, then the risks of relying on them for just the next ten, twenty, or fifty years is relatively low compared to the benefits of having a formula that appears to work but is not fully understandable (and that, at the very least, might prove useful in helping scientists cure genetic diseases).
当然，鉴于我们大脑的容量和处理速度有限，我们可能需要很长时间才能对进化中的所有变量有丰富的理解。我们在专家系统中采用的所有简化和理解是否真的需要？也许不是。当然，仍然存在非测试数据中的更改可能仍会发生的风险。但有人可能会争辩说，如果我们基于数据挖掘的公式似乎能够解释所有物种在所有时间的进化，那么与拥有一个似乎有效但不能完全理解的公式的好处相比，在接下来的十年、二十年或五十年内依赖它们的风险相对较低（而且， 至少，可能被证明有助于帮助科学家治愈遗传疾病）。

In fact, we may be too hung up on understanding; conscious thinking is only one part of understanding. Maybe it’s enough that we derive a formula for change and use it to anticipate what is yet to come. I myself find the excitement, lower risk, and educational value of achieving a deep understanding of cause-effect relationships much more appealing than a reliance on algorithms I don’t understand, so I am drawn to that path. But is it my lower-level preferences and habits that are pulling me in this direction or is it my logic and reason? I’m not sure. I look forward to probing the best minds in artificial intelligence on this (and having them probe me).
事实上，我们可能太执着于理解;有意识的思考只是理解的一部分。也许我们得出一个变革的公式并使用它来预测即将发生的事情就足够了。我自己发现，与依赖我不理解的算法相比，深入了解因果关系的兴奋、降低风险和教育价值要吸引人得多，所以我被这条路所吸引。但是，是我较低层次的偏好和习惯将我拉向这个方向，还是我的逻辑和理性？我不确定。我期待着在这方面探索人工智能领域最优秀的人才（并让他们调查我）。

Most likely, our competitive natures will compel us to place bigger and bigger bets on relationships computers find that are beyond our understanding. Some of those bets will pay off, while others will backfire. I suspect that AI will lead to incredibly fast and remarkable advances, but I also fear that it could lead to our demise.
最有可能的是，我们的竞争天性会迫使我们在计算机发现的超出我们理解范围的关系上下越来越大的赌注。其中一些赌注会得到回报，而另一些则会适得其反。我怀疑人工智能会带来令人难以置信的快速和显着的进步，但我也担心它可能导致我们的灭亡。

We are headed for an exciting and perilous new world. That’s our reality. And as always, I believe that we are much better off preparing to deal with it than wishing it weren’t true.
我们正走向一个令人兴奋但又危险的新世界。这就是我们的现实。一如既往，我相信我们准备应对它比希望它不是真的要好得多。

In order to have the best life possible, you have to:
为了过上最好的生活，您必须：

1) know what the best decisions are and
1） 知道最佳决策是什么，以及

2) have the courage to make them.
2） 有勇气去做它们。

THE ULTIMATE BOON:
终极恩惠：

1995–2010

By 1995, Bridgewater had grown to forty-two employees and $4.1 billion under management, which was more than I’d ever hoped for, especially considering that Bridgewater had been down to just me only a dozen years before. While things were much better and more stable, we were still doing basically the same things I’d been doing from the start—wrestling with the markets, thinking independently and creatively about how to make our bets, making mistakes, bringing those mistakes to the surface, diagnosing them to get at their root causes, designing new and better ways of doing things, systematically implementing the changes, making new mistakes, and so on.4 This iterative, evolutionary approach allowed us to continually refine the investment systems that I’d begun building in 1982. Back then, we showed that a few bright guys with computers could beat the big, well-equipped establishment players. Now we were becoming the well-equipped establishment ourselves.
到1995 年，Bridgewater 已经发展到 42 名员工，管理着 41 亿美元，这超出了我的预期，尤其是考虑到 Bridgewater 在十几年前只剩下我一个人。虽然情况好多了，也稳定多了，但我们仍然在做我从一开始就做的基本相同的事情——与市场搏斗，独立和创造性地思考如何下注，犯错误，将这些错误浮出水面，诊断它们以找到根本原因，设计新的、更好的做事方式。 系统地实施更改、犯新错误等。4 这种迭代、渐进的方法使我们能够不断完善我从 1982 年开始构建的投资系统。那时，我们证明，一些拥有计算机的聪明人可以击败高大、装备精良的建制派玩家。现在我们自己也成为了装备精良的机构。

As the number of decision rules and the amount of data in our systems grew more complex, we hired young programmers who were better than us in converting our instructions into code and smart new grads right out of college to help with our investment research. One of these new whiz kids, Greg Jensen, joined Bridgewater as a college intern in 1996. Because he shined, I grabbed him as my research assistant. Over the decades that followed, he contributed a lot, grew into the co-chief investment officer role with Bob Prince and me, and became a co-CEO. He also became like a godson to me.
随着决策规则的数量和系统中的数据量变得越来越复杂，我们聘请了比我们更擅长将指令转换为代码的年轻程序员，以及刚从大学毕业的聪明的应届毕业生来帮助我们进行投资研究。这些新奇子之一 Greg Jensen 于 1996 年作为大学实习生加入布里奇沃特。因为他很出色，所以我找他做我的研究助理。在接下来的几十年里，他做出了很多贡献，与我和 Bob Prince 一起成长为联合首席投资官，并成为联合首席执行官。他也变得像我的教子一样。

We also invested in more and more powerful computers.5 Having our systems running through these machines freed us to get above the daily movements of the markets and consider things from a higher level, where we could make novel, creative connections that produced innovations for our clients.
我们还投资了越来越强大的计算机。5 通过这些机器运行我们的系统，我们可以超越市场的日常波动，从更高的层次考虑问题，在那里我们可以建立新颖、创造性的联系，为我们的客户带来创新。

DISCOVERING INFLATION-INDEXED BONDS
发现通胀指数债券

Around this time, I had dinner with David White, the man in charge of the Rockefeller Foundation’s money. David asked me how I would engineer the foundation’s portfolio to produce a return that was 5 percent above the U.S. inflation rate. I answered that a portfolio of leveraged foreign inflation-indexed bonds with the currency hedged back to U.S. dollars should deliver exactly that. (The bonds needed to be foreign because there were no U.S. inflation-indexed bonds at the time, and they needed to be hedged to the dollar so there would be no currency risk.)
大约在这个时候，我和负责洛克菲勒基金会资金的大卫·怀特共进晚餐。David 问我如何设计基金会的投资组合，以产生比美国通货膨胀率高 5% 的回报。我回答说，将货币对冲回美元且杠杆外国通胀指数债券投资组合应该能做到这一点。（这些债券必须是外国债券，因为当时没有美国通胀指数债券，而且它们需要对冲美元，这样就不会有货币风险。

Thinking about this later, I realized that we could create an entirely new and radically different asset class, so Dan Bernstein and I researched such a portfolio more closely. According to our analysis, this new asset class would perform even better than we’d thought. In fact, it would be uniquely effective because we could engineer it to have the same expected return as equities but with less risk and with a negative correlation with bonds and equities over long time frames. We showed this research to our clients and they loved it. Before long, we became the first global inflation-indexed bond manager in the world. In 1996, U.S. Treasury deputy secretary Larry Summers began looking into whether the U.S. should issue its own inflation-indexed bonds, and because we were the only manager with a portfolio of such bonds, he called us in as experts.
后来想了想，我意识到我们可以创造一种全新的、截然不同的资产类别，因此 Dan Bernstein 和我更仔细地研究了这样一个投资组合。根据我们的分析，这种新资产类别的表现将比我们想象的还要好。事实上，这将具有独特的效果，因为我们可以将其设计为具有与股票相同的预期回报，但风险较小，并且在长期范围内与债券和股票呈负相关。我们向客户展示了这项研究，他们很喜欢。不久，我们成为全球首家全球通胀指数债券管理公司。1996 年，美国财政部副部长拉里·萨默斯 （Larry Summers） 开始研究美国是否应该发行自己的通胀指数债券，因为我们是唯一拥有此类债券投资组合的管理人，所以他请我们担任专家。

Dan and I traveled down to Washington to meet with Summers, his Treasury colleagues, and a number of representatives from well-known Wall Street firms. We were late (punctuality isn’t one of my strengths) and the doors to the big meeting room at Treasury were locked. I wasn’t going to let that stop me, so I knocked until someone opened it. It was a large room with a table in the middle and a press gallery off to the side. There was only one seat open at the table and it had Dan’s nameplate in front of it—we’d agreed that he’d be our one allowed representative since he’d done a lot of the prep work. I had forgotten that, so I walked over to the press gallery, grabbed a chair, and carried it next to Dan’s so I had a seat at the table too. Dan describes that meeting as an analogy for what it was like for us in the 1990s in general: We had to barge our way into things. Larry Summers has since said that the advice he got from us was the most important in shaping this market. When the Treasury did create the bonds, they followed the structure we recommended.
丹和我前往华盛顿，会见了萨默斯、他的财政部同事以及一些来自华尔街知名公司的代表。我们迟到了（守时不是我的强项之一），Treasury 大会议室的门也锁上了。我不想让它阻止我，所以我敲门直到有人打开它。这是一个大房间，中间有一张桌子，旁边有一个新闻发布厅。桌子上只有一个空位，前面有 Dan 的铭牌——我们同意他将成为我们唯一允许的代表，因为他做了很多准备工作。我忘了这件事，所以我走到新闻发布厅，拿了一把椅子，把它搬到 Dan 的旁边，这样我也在桌子旁坐下了。Dan 将那次会议描述为 1990 年代我们总体情况的类比：我们不得不闯入事物。拉里·萨默斯 （Larry Summers） 此后表示，他从我们那里得到的建议对于塑造这个市场来说是最重要的。当财政部确实发行债券时，他们遵循了我们建议的结构。

DISCOVERING RISK PARITY
发现风险平价

By the mid-1990s, I had enough money to set up a trust for my family, so I began to think about what the best asset allocation mix for preserving wealth over generations would look like. In my years as an investor, I had seen all sorts of economic and market environments and all kinds of ways that wealth could be created and destroyed. I knew what drove asset returns, but I also knew that no matter what asset class one held, there would come a time when it would lose most of its value. This included cash, which is the worst investment over time because it loses value after adjusting for inflation and taxes. I also knew how difficult it was to anticipate the swings that cause those losses. I’ve devoted my life to it and I’ve made my share of bad calls; anticipating these swings wasn’t something I’d bet on others doing well when I wasn’t around. Finding investors who have done well in all economic environments—when inflation rises and when it falls, when there are booms and when there are busts—is like finding needles in a haystack, and they don’t live forever so that’s not a viable path. I didn’t want the wealth I had created to protect my family to be wiped out after I was gone. That meant I had to create a mix of assets that could be good in all economic environments.
到 1990 年代中期，我有足够的钱为我的家人设立信托，所以我开始思考为几代人保存财富的最佳资产配置组合会是什么样子。在我作为投资者的这些年里，我见过各种各样的经济和市场环境，以及创造和销毁财富的各种方式。我知道是什么推动了资产回报，但我也知道，无论持有哪种资产类别，总有一天它会失去大部分价值。这包括现金，随着时间的推移，这是最糟糕的投资，因为它在调整通货膨胀和税收后会贬值。我也知道预测导致这些损失的波动是多么困难。我把我的一生都献给了它，我也做过我自己的坏决定;预测这些波动并不是我敢打赌其他人在我不在的时候做得很好的事情。寻找在所有经济环境中都表现良好的投资者——当通胀上升和下降时，当有繁荣和萧条时——就像大海捞针一样，他们不会永远活着，所以这不是一条可行的道路。我不希望我为保护家人而创造的财富在我离开后被抹去。这意味着我必须创建在所有经济环境中都适用的资产组合。

I knew which shifts in the economic environment caused asset classes to move around, and I knew that those relationships had remained essentially the same for hundreds of years. There were only two big forces to worry about: growth and inflation. Each could either be rising or falling, so I saw that by finding four different investment strategies—each one of which would do well in a particular environment (rising growth with rising inflation, rising growth with falling inflation, and so on)—I could construct an asset-allocation mix that was balanced to do well over time while being protected against unacceptable losses. Since that strategy would never change, practically anyone could implement it. And so, with help from Bob and Dan, I created a portfolio mix that I could comfortably put my trust money in for the next hundred or more years. I called it the “All Weather Portfolio” because it could perform well in all environments.
我知道经济环境的哪些变化导致资产类别发生变化，我知道这些关系数百年来基本保持不变。只有两大力量需要担心：增长和通货膨胀。每种策略都可以上涨或下跌，所以我看到，通过找到四种不同的投资策略——每一种在特定环境中都会表现良好（通胀上升时增长上升，通胀下降时增长上升，等等）我可以构建一个资产配置组合，该组合随着时间的推移表现良好，同时免受不可接受的损失。由于该策略永远不会改变，因此几乎任何人都可以实施它。因此，在 Bob 和 Dan 的帮助下，我创建了一个投资组合，我可以在接下来的一百年或更长时间内轻松地将我的信托资金投入其中。我称它为“全天候产品组合”，因为它可以在所有环境中表现良好。

Between 1996 and 2003 I was the only “client” investing in it because we didn’t sell it as a product. But in 2003, the head of Verizon’s pension fund, a longtime client, told us he was looking for an approach to investing that would do well across all environments. After Verizon made its investment, others quickly followed, and a dozen years later we were managing nearly $80 billion. It was another industry-shaping concept. Seeing its success, other investment managers followed with their own versions. It is now generically called “risk parity” investing.
在 1996 年至 2003 年期间，我是唯一投资它的“客户”，因为我们没有将其作为产品销售。但在 2003 年，Verizon 养老基金的负责人（一位长期客户）告诉我们，他正在寻找一种在所有环境中都能表现良好的投资方法。在 Verizon 进行投资后，其他公司很快跟进，十几年后，我们管理着近 800 亿美元。这是另一个塑造行业的概念。看到它的成功，其他投资经理纷纷效仿，推出了自己的版本。它现在通常被称为“风险平价”投资。

TO REMAIN A BEAUTIFUL BOUTIQUE OR BECOME A GREAT INSTITUTION?
继续成为一家美丽的精品店还是成为一个伟大的机构？

With our people and culture producing these industry-shaping investment products, Bridgewater really took off. By 2000, we were managing more than $32 billion, almost eight times what we had been managing just five years before. Our head count had doubled, so we moved out of our strip mall office into a larger space situated in a nature preserve on the banks of the Saugatuck River. But while we continued to grow, it was never clear sailing. Building the business while managing investments required me to do two challenging jobs simultaneously and develop two distinct skill sets, while being a good father, husband, and friend. The demands of these roles changed over time, so the skills and abilities I needed changed as well.
凭借我们的员工和文化生产出这些塑造行业的投资产品，Bridgewater 真正起飞了。到 2000 年，我们管理着超过 320 亿美元的资产，几乎是五年前管理的八倍。我们的员工人数翻了一番，因此我们搬出了我们的商业街办公室，搬到了位于索格塔克河（Saugatuck River）河畔自然保护区的一个更大的空间。但是，虽然我们继续增长，但从来都不是一帆风顺的。在管理投资的同时建立业务需要我同时做两项具有挑战性的工作并发展两种不同的技能，同时成为一个好父亲、好丈夫和好朋友。这些角色的要求随着时间的推移而变化，因此我需要的技能和能力也发生了变化。

Most people assume that the challenges that go along with growing a large business are greater than those of growing a smaller one. That is not true. Going from a five-person organization to a sixty-person organization was just as challenging as going from a sixty-person organization to a seven-hundred-person organization—and from a seven-hundred-person organization to a 1,500-person one. Looking back, I can’t say that the challenges were easier or harder at any of the various phases we went through. They were just different. For example, when I had no one to manage, I had the challenge of having to do almost everything myself. When I learned and earned enough to pay others, I had the challenge of managing them. Similarly, the challenges of wrestling with market and economic swings were constantly changing. I didn’t think about it then, but it’s obvious to me now that while one gets better at things over time, it doesn’t become any easier if one is also progressing to higher levels—the Olympic athlete finds his sport to be every bit as challenging as the novice does.
大多数人认为，发展大型企业所面临的挑战比发展小型企业所面临的挑战更大。这不是真的。从一个 5 人的组织变成一个 60 人的组织，就像从一个 60 人的组织变成一个 700 人的组织，以及从一个 700 人的组织变成一个 1500 人的组织一样具有挑战性。回想起来，我不能说在我们经历的各个阶段中，挑战是更容易还是更难。他们只是不同。例如，当我没有人需要管理时，我面临着几乎必须自己做所有事情的挑战。当我学到并赚到足够的钱来支付别人的钱时，我就面临着管理他们的挑战。同样，与市场和经济波动作斗争的挑战也在不断变化。我当时没有想过，但现在对我来说很明显，虽然一个人随着时间的推移会变得更好，但如果一个人也进步到更高的水平，那也不会变得更容易——奥林匹克运动员发现他的运动和新手一样具有挑战性。

Very soon we faced another critical choice: What kind of company did we want to have? Should we continue to grow or stay about the same size?
很快，我们面临着另一个关键的选择：我们想拥有什么样的公司？我们应该继续增长还是保持相同的大小？

By 2003, I had come to believe that we needed to grow Bridgewater into a real institution instead of remaining a typical boutique-sized investment manager. Doing this would make us better in many ways—better technology, better security controls, a deeper talent pool—all of which would make us more stable and permanent. This meant hiring more people in technology, infrastructure, and other areas, as well as additional HR and IT staff to train and support them.
到 2003 年，我开始相信我们需要将 Bridgewater 发展成为一家真正的机构，而不是继续成为典型的精品规模的投资管理公司。这样做会让我们在很多方面变得更好——更好的技术、更好的安全控制、更深层次的人才库——所有这些都将使我们更加稳定和持久。这意味着在技术、基础设施和其他领域雇用更多人员，以及额外的 HR 和 IT 人员来培训和支持他们。

Giselle argued strongly that we should not grow. She believed that introducing a lot of new people would threaten our culture, and that the time and attention that hiring, training, and managing them required would dilute our focus. While I agreed with her points, I didn’t like the alternative of not allowing ourselves to become all we could be. I felt about this fork-in-the-road choice the way I felt about most others—that whether or not we could have our cake and eat it too was merely a test of our creativity and character. For example, I could envision ways in which technology would help us get the most out of people. After a fair amount of wrestling with these questions, we decided to go ahead.
吉赛尔强烈主张我们不应该增长。她认为引入大量新人会威胁到我们的文化，而招聘、培训和管理他们所需的时间和精力会分散我们的注意力。虽然我同意她的观点，但我不喜欢不允许我们成为我们能成为的一切。我对这个岔路口的选择的感觉和我对大多数人的感受一样——我们是否可以拥有我们的蛋糕并也能吃掉它，这只是对我们的创造力和性格的考验。例如，我可以设想技术如何帮助我们充分利用人们。在对这些问题进行了相当多的纠结之后，我们决定继续进行。

FLESHING OUT PRINCIPLES
充实原则

Ever since Bob, Giselle, and Dan had presented me with the “Ray Feedback Memo” in the 1990s, I had been much more explicit in writing down and sharing my work principles in the same way I had written down my investment principles. At first, this took the form of shared philosophy statements and emails to the entire company. Then, whenever something new came along that required me to make a decision, I would reflect on my criteria for making that decision and write it down as a principle so people could make the connections between the situation, my principle for handling these situations, and my actions. More and more, we saw everything as “another one of those”—another of a certain type of situation like hiring, firing, determining compensation, dealing with dishonesty—that had principles for handling them. By having them explicitly written out, I could foster the idea meritocracy by having us together reflect on and refine those principles—and then adhere to them.
自从 Bob、Giselle 和 Dan 在 1990 年代向我提交“Ray 反馈备忘录”以来，我就更加明确地写下和分享我的工作原则，就像我写下我的投资原则一样。起初，这采取了向整个公司分享理念声明和电子邮件的形式。然后，每当出现需要我做出决定的新事物时，我就会反思我做出该决定的标准并将其作为一项原则写下来，以便人们能够在情况、我处理这些情况的原则和我的行动之间建立联系。我们越来越多地将一切都视为“其中的另一种”——另一种特定类型的情况，如招聘、解雇、确定薪酬、处理不诚实行为——都有处理它们的原则。通过明确写下这些原则，我可以让我们一起反思和完善这些原则，然后遵守这些原则，从而培养精英政治的理念。

The number of principles started small and grew over time. By the mid-2000s, Bridgewater was beginning to grow rapidly, and we had a number of new managers trying to learn and adapt to our unique culture—and who were increasingly asking me for advice. I was also beginning to have people from outside Bridgewater ask me how they could create idea meritocracies of their own. So in 2006, I prepared a rough list of about sixty Work Principles and distributed them to Bridgewater’s managers so they could begin to evaluate them, debate them, and make sense of them for themselves. “It’s a rough draft,” I wrote in the covering memo, “but it is being put out now for comments.”
原则的数量开始时很小，然后随着时间的推移而增加。到 2000 年代中期，Bridgewater 开始迅速发展，我们有许多新经理试图学习和适应我们独特的文化，他们越来越多地向我寻求建议。我也开始有来自布里奇沃特以外的人问我，他们如何创造自己的思想精英制。因此，在 2006 年，我准备了一份包含 60 条工作原则的粗略清单，并将它们分发给 Bridgewater 的经理，以便他们开始评估、辩论并自己理解它们。“这是一份粗略的草稿，”我在封面备忘录中写道，“但现在正在公布征求意见。

This began an ongoing evolutionary process of encountering many situations, forming principles about how to deal with them, and getting in sync with other Bridgewater leaders and managers about them. Over time, I encountered most everything there is to encounter in running a company, so I had a few hundred principles that covered most everything. That collection of principles, like our collection of investment principles, became a kind of decision-making library. Those principles are the basis of what you’ll find in Work Principles.
这开始了一个持续的进化过程，遇到许多情况，形成如何处理它们的原则，并与其他 Bridgewater 的领导者和管理者保持同步。随着时间的推移，我几乎遇到了经营公司时遇到的所有事情，所以我有几百条原则，几乎涵盖了所有内容。这些原则集合，就像我们的投资原则集合一样，成为一种决策库。这些原则是您将在 Work Principles 中找到的基础。

But it wasn’t enough to codify and teach our philosophy; we had to live it. As the company grew bigger, how that happened evolved. In Bridgewater’s early days, everyone knew each other, so being radically transparent was easy—people could attend the meetings they wanted to and communicate with each other informally. But as we grew, that became logistically impossible, which was a real problem. How could people engage productively with the idea meritocracy if they didn’t know everything that was going on? Without transparency, people would spin whatever happened to suit their own interests, sometimes behind closed doors. Problems would be hidden instead of brought to the surface where they could be resolved. To have a real idea meritocracy, there must be transparency so that people can see things for themselves.
但这还不够编纂和教授我们的哲学;我们必须活出它。随着公司的发展壮大，这种情况发生了变化。在 Bridgewater 的早期，每个人都彼此认识，因此很容易做到完全透明——人们可以参加他们想参加的会议，并进行非正式的交流。但随着我们的发展，这在物流上变得不可能，这是一个真正的问题。如果人们不知道正在发生的一切，他们怎么能有效地参与到精英政治的理念中呢？没有透明度，人们会为了自己的利益而捏造任何事情，有时甚至是关起门来。问题将被隐藏起来，而不是浮出水面来解决。要有一个真正的理念任人唯贤，必须有透明度，这样人们才能自己看到事情。

To make sure this happened, I required that virtually all our meetings be recorded and made available to everyone, with extremely rare exceptions such as when we were discussing very private matters like personal health or proprietary information about a trade or decision rule. At first I sent these tapes of management meetings unedited to the entire company, but that was a huge burden on people’s time. So I built a small team to edit the tapes, focusing on the most important moments, and over time we added questions to create “virtual reality” case studies that could be used for training.6 Over time, these tapes became part of a “boot camp” for new employees as well as a window into an ongoing stream of situations connected to the principles for handling them.
为了确保这一点发生，我要求我们几乎所有的会议都被记录下来并提供给所有人，除了极少数的例外，例如当我们讨论非常私人的问题时，例如个人健康或有关交易或决策规则的专有信息。起初，我把这些未经编辑的管理会议录音带发给了整个公司，但这对人们的时间来说是一个巨大的负担。因此，我组建了一个小团队来编辑磁带，专注于最重要的时刻，随着时间的推移，我们添加了问题以创建可用于培训的“虚拟现实”案例研究。6 随着时间的推移，这些磁带成为新员工“新兵训练营”的一部分，也是了解与处理原则相关的持续情况的窗口。

All this openness led to some very frank discussions about who did what and why, and as a result we were able to deepen our understanding of our different ways of thinking. This was enlightening to all of us in showing how differently people’s brains worked. If nothing else, I could better appreciate people I’d once wanted to strangle! Moreover, I recognized that managers who do not understand people’s different thinking styles cannot understand how the people working for them will handle different situations, which is like a foreman not understanding how his equipment will behave. That insight led us to explore psychometric testing as a way of learning how people think differently.
所有这些开放性导致了一些非常坦率的讨论，关于谁做了什么以及为什么做，因此我们能够加深对我们不同思维方式的理解。这对我们所有人来说都是启发性的，因为它展示了人们的大脑工作方式是多么不同。如果不出意外，我可以更好地欣赏我曾经想掐死的人！此外，我认识到，不了解人们不同思维方式的管理者无法理解为他们工作的人将如何处理不同的情况，这就像工头不了解他的设备将如何表现一样。这种洞察力促使我们探索心理测试，将其作为了解人们不同思维方式的一种方式。

DISCOVERING PSYCHOMETRIC TESTING
探索心理测试

Early in my kids’ lives, I’d had them tested by a brilliant psychologist named Sue Quinlan. Her assessments proved spot-on and provided a great road map for how they would develop over the years. Because that testing process had been so successful, I worked with her and others to try to identify the best tests for determining what the people I worked with were like. In 2006, I took the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) assessment for the first time and found its description of my preferences to be remarkably accurate.
在我孩子生命的早期，我曾让一位名叫 Sue Quinlan 的才华横溢的心理学家对他们进行测试。事实证明，她的评估是准确的，并为他们多年来的发展提供了很好的路线图。因为那个测试过程非常成功，所以我与她和其他人一起尝试确定最好的测试，以确定与我一起工作的人是什么样的。2006 年，我第一次参加了迈尔斯-布里格斯类型指标 （MBTI） 评估，发现它对我偏好的描述非常准确。

Many of the differences it described, such as those between “intuiting people,” who tend to focus on big-picture concepts, and “sensing people,” who pay more attention to specific facts and details, were highly relevant to the conflicts and disagreements we were having at Bridgewater. I began to look for other tests that could help us deepen our understanding of each other. This was slow going at first, largely because most of the psychologists I met were surprisingly squeamish about exploring differences. But eventually I found a few great people, especially a psychologist named Bob Eichinger, who pointed me to a number of other very useful tests.
它描述的许多差异，例如倾向于关注大局概念的“直觉人”和更关注具体事实和细节的“感知人”之间的差异，与我们在桥水学院遇到的冲突和分歧高度相关。我开始寻找其他可以帮助我们加深彼此理解的测试。起初进展缓慢，主要是因为我遇到的大多数心理学家都出奇地热衷于探索差异。但最终我找到了一些很棒的人，尤其是一位名叫 Bob Eichinger 的心理学家，他向我推荐了许多其他非常有用的测试。

In early 2008, I had most of Bridgewater’s managers take the Myers-Briggs assessment. The results astounded me. I couldn’t believe that some of them actually thought in the ways the test described, yet when I asked them to rate how well it described them on a scale of one to five, more than 80 percent of them gave it a four or five.
2008 年初，我让 Bridgewater 的大多数经理参加了 Myers-Briggs 评估。结果让我感到震惊。我不敢相信他们中的一些人真的按照测试描述的方式思考，但是当我让他们用 1 到 5 的等级来评价测试对他们的描述时，超过 80% 的人给了 4 或 5 分。

CREATING BASEBALL CARDS
创建棒球卡

Even after we were armed with the Myers-Briggs data and other tests we’d taken, I found that we were still having a hard time connecting the dots between the outcomes that we were seeing and what we knew about the people producing them. Over and over again, the same people would walk into the same meetings, do things the same ways, and get the same results without seeking to understand why. (Recently I came across a study that revealed a cognitive bias in which people consistently overlook the evidence of one person being better than another at something and assume that both are equally good at a task. This was exactly what we were seeing.) For example, people who were known not to be creative were being assigned tasks that required creativity; people who didn’t pay attention to details were being assigned to detail-oriented jobs, and so on. We needed a way to make the data that showed what people were like even clearer and more explicit, so I began making “Baseball Cards” for employees that listed their “stats.” The idea was that they could be passed around and referred to when assigning responsibilities. Just as you wouldn’t have a great fielder with a .160 batting average bat third, you wouldn’t assign a big-picture person a task requiring attention to details.
即使在我们掌握了 Myers-Briggs 数据和其他测试之后，我发现我们仍然很难将我们所看到的结果与我们对产生这些结果的人的了解联系起来。一遍又一遍，相同的人会走进相同的会议，以相同的方式做事，并得到相同的结果，而无需试图理解原因。（最近我看到一项研究，揭示了一种认知偏差，即人们总是忽视一个人在某件事上比另一个人更好的证据，并认为两人在某项任务上同样擅长。这正是我们所看到的。例如，已知没有创造力的人被分配了需要创造力的任务;不注意细节的人被分配到注重细节的工作，依此类推。我们需要一种方法来使数据更清晰、更明确地显示人们的情况，因此我开始为员工制作“棒球卡”，列出他们的“统计数据”。这个想法是，在分配职责时可以传递和引用它们。就像你不会有一个平均击球率为 .160 的优秀外野手一样，你也不会给一个大局观的人分配一个需要注意细节的任务。

At first, this idea met a lot of resistance. People were concerned that the Baseball Cards wouldn’t be accurate, that producing them would be too time-consuming, and that they would only succeed in pigeonholing people unfairly. But over time, everyone’s attitudes toward this approach of openly exploring what people are like shifted 180 degrees. Most people found that having this information out in the open for everyone to see was more liberating than constraining because when it became the norm, people gained the sort of comfort that comes with just being themselves at work that family members have with each other at home.
起初，这个想法遇到了很多阻力。人们担心棒球卡不准确，制作它们会太耗时，而且他们只会成功地不公平地将人们归类。但随着时间的推移，每个人对这种公开探索人们是什么样的方法的态度发生了 180 度大转弯。大多数人发现，将这些信息公开给所有人看比束缚更自由，因为当它成为常态时，人们获得了在工作中做自己所带来的那种舒适感，就像家庭成员在家里彼此相处一样。

Because this way of operating was so unusual, a number of behavioral psychologists came to Bridgewater to evaluate it. I urge you to read their assessments, which were overwhelmingly favorable.7 The Harvard psychologist Bob Kegan called Bridgewater “a form of proof that the quest for business excellence and the search for personal realization need not be mutually exclusive—and can, in fact, be essential to each other.”
由于这种运作方式非常不寻常，许多行为心理学家来到布里奇沃特进行评估。我敦促您阅读他们的评估，这些评估绝大多数是有利的。“7 哈佛大学心理学家鲍勃·凯根 （Bob Kegan） 称布里奇沃特案是”一种证明形式，证明追求卓越的商业成就和追求个人实现不一定是相互排斥的——事实上，它们对彼此来说是必不可少的。

I should also explain that my personal circumstances at the time also drew me to psychology and neurology. While for the most part I am keeping my family members’ lives out of this book to protect their privacy, I will tell you this one story about my son Paul as it is relevant and he is open about it.
我还应该解释一下，我当时的个人情况也吸引了我去学习心理学和神经学。虽然在大多数情况下，我把家人的生活排除在这本书之外以保护他们的隐私，但我会告诉你这个关于我儿子保罗的故事，因为它是相关的，他对此持开放态度。

After graduating from NYU’s Tisch film school, Paul headed out to Los Angeles to take a job. One day he went to the front desk of the hotel where he was staying while he looked for an apartment and smashed their computer. He was arrested and thrown in jail, where he was beaten up by guards. Ultimately, he was diagnosed with bipolar disorder, released into my custody, and admitted to the psychiatric ward of a hospital.
从纽约大学 Tisch 电影学院毕业后，Paul 前往洛杉矶找了一份工作。有一天，他去他住的酒店的前台找公寓，砸碎了他们的电脑。他被捕并被关进监狱，在那里他被警卫殴打。最终，他被诊断出患有躁郁症，被释放到我的监护下，并被送进了一家医院的精神病房。

That was the beginning of a three-year roller-coaster ride that took Paul, Barbara, and me to the peaks of his manias and the depths of his depressions, through the twists and turns of the health care system, and into discussions with some of the most brilliant and caring psychologists, psychiatrists, and neuroscientists at work today. There is nothing to prompt learning like pain and necessity, and this gave me plenty of both. At times I felt as though I was holding Paul by the hand as he was dangling over a cliff—from one day to the next, I never knew whether I could hold on or if he would slip from my grip. I worked intensely with his caregivers to understand what was going on and what to do about it. Thanks both to the help he received and his own great character, Paul worked through this and is now better off than if he hadn’t fallen into his abyss, because he developed strengths he didn’t have but needed. Paul was once wild—staying out till all hours, disorganized, smoking marijuana and drinking—but he now faithfully takes his meds, meditates, goes to bed early, and avoids drugs and alcohol. He had loads of creativity but lacked discipline. Now he has plenty of both. As a result, he is more creative now than he was before and is happily married, the father of two boys, an accomplished filmmaker, and a crusader helping those who struggle with bipolar disorder.
那是我三年过山车之旅的开始，保罗、芭芭拉和我走到了他躁狂症的顶峰和抑郁的深处，穿过了医疗保健系统的曲折，并与当今工作中一些最聪明、最有爱心的心理学家、精神病学家和神经科学家进行了讨论。没有什么比痛苦和必要性更能促使学习的了，这给了我很多这两者。有时我觉得自己好像在保罗悬崖上晃来晃去——从一天到第二天，我永远不知道我是否能坚持下去，或者他是否会从我的手中溜走。我与他的护理人员密切合作，以了解发生了什么以及如何应对。多亏了他得到的帮助和他自己的伟大品格，保罗度过了这一切，现在比他没有掉进深渊时要好，因为他发展了他没有但需要的力量。保罗曾经很狂野——整天在外面呆着，杂乱无章，抽大麻和喝酒——但现在他忠实地吃药、打药、早睡，避免吸毒和酗酒。他有很多创造力，但缺乏纪律。现在他两者都有很多。因此，他现在比以前更有创造力，婚姻幸福，是两个男孩的父亲，是一位多才多艺的电影制作人，也是一名帮助那些与躁郁症作斗争的人的斗士。

His radical transparency about being bipolar and his commitment to helping others with it inspires me. His first feature film, Touched with Fire, which received lots of acclaim, gave many people who might have lost their lives to bipolar disorder both the hope and the path forward they needed. I remember watching him shoot one scene based on a real conversation between us in which he was manic and I was struggling to reason with him. I could simultaneously see the actor playing Paul at his worst while the real Paul was at his best, directing the scene. As I watched, my mind flashed over his whole journey—from the depths of his abyss, through his metamorphosis into the strong hero standing in front of me, someone on a mission to help others going through what he had gone through.
他对躁郁症的彻底透明以及他帮助他人的承诺激励了我。他的第一部长片《Touched with Fire》获得了很多好评，给许多可能因躁郁症而丧生的人带来了他们需要的希望和前进的道路。我记得我看他根据我们之间的真实对话拍摄了一个场景，他很狂躁，我正在努力与他讲道理。我可以同时看到演员扮演保罗最糟糕的时候，而真正的保罗则处于最佳状态，导演了这个场景。当我看着时，我的脑海中闪过了他的整个旅程——从他的深渊，到他蜕变成站在我面前的强大英雄，一个肩负着帮助他人经历他所经历的使命的人。

That journey through hell gave me a much deeper understanding of how and why we see things differently. I learned that much of how we think is physiological and can be changed. For example, Paul’s wild swings were due to the inconsistent secretions of dopamine and other chemicals in his brain, so he could change by controlling those chemicals and the activities and stimuli affecting them. I learned that creative genius and insanity can be quite close to each other, that the same chemistry that creates insights can cause distortions, and that being stuck in one’s own head is terribly dangerous. When Paul was “crazy,” he always believed his own illogical arguments, no matter how strange they sounded to others. While more extreme in the case of someone with bipolar disorder, this is something I’ve seen most everyone do. I also learned how people can control how their brains work to produce dramatically better effects. These insights helped me to deal with people more effectively, as I will explain in detail in Chapter Four, Understand That People Are Wired Very Differently.
那次穿越地狱的旅程让我对我们如何以及为什么以不同的方式看待事物有了更深入的理解。我了解到，我们的思维方式在很大程度上是生理性的，是可以改变的。例如，Paul 的剧烈波动是由于大脑中多巴胺和其他化学物质的分泌不一致，因此他可以通过控制这些化学物质以及影响它们的活动和刺激来改变。我了解到，创造性的天才和疯狂可以非常接近，创造洞察力的相同化学反应会导致扭曲，而被困在自己的头脑中是非常危险的。当保罗 “疯了” 时，他总是相信自己不合逻辑的论点，无论它们在别人看来多么奇怪。虽然在双相情感障碍患者的情况下更极端，但这是我见过大多数人都会做的事情。我还了解了人们如何控制他们的大脑如何工作以产生显着更好的效果。这些见解帮助我更有效地与人打交道，正如我将在第四章中详细解释的那样，理解人们的性格非常不同。

MAKING BRIDGEWATER ROCK-SOLID AND CUTTING-EDGE
让布里奇沃特坚如磐石和尖端

At our annual town hall meeting in June 2008, I said that seen through my eyes Bridgewater was then, and always had been, “both terrible and terrific at the same time.” After about five years of rapid growth toward building Bridgewater as an institution, we had encountered our newest set of problems. This was nothing new. Since I started Bridgewater we always had some problems because we were always doing bold new things, making mistakes, and evolving quickly. For example, technology had changed so quickly during the years we’d built the company that we had literally switched from using slide rules to spreadsheet software to advanced artificial intelligence. With so much changing so fast, it had seemed pointless to focus on getting everything “just right” when something newer and better was sure to come along. So we built our technology in a light and flexible way, which made sense at the time but also created a number of hairballs that badly needed untangling. That same approach of moving quickly and flexibly had been true throughout the company, so several departments had become overstretched as we grew. It had always been fun being cutting-edge, but we were having a hard time becoming rock-solid, especially in the noninvestment side of the business. The organization needed to be renovated in several ways—but it wasn’t going to be easy.
在 2008 年 6 月的年度市政厅会议上，我说，在我眼中，布里奇沃特当时和过去都是“既可怕又了不起”。在将布里奇沃特大学建设为一所机构的大约五年的快速发展之后，我们遇到了最新的一系列问题。这并不是什么新鲜事。自从我创办 Bridgewater 以来，我们总是遇到一些问题，因为我们总是在做大胆的新事物，犯错误，并且发展迅速。例如，在我们建立公司的这些年里，技术变化如此之快，以至于我们实际上已经从使用计算尺转向使用电子表格软件，再到高级人工智能。变化如此之快，当更新更好的东西肯定会出现时，专注于让一切都“恰到好处”似乎毫无意义。因此，我们以一种轻松灵活的方式构建了我们的技术，这在当时是有道理的，但也产生了许多急需解开的毛球。这种快速灵活行动的方法在整个公司都适用，因此随着我们的发展，几个部门已经不堪重负。成为前沿一直很有趣，但我们很难变得坚如磐石，尤其是在非投资业务方面。该组织需要以多种方式进行翻新，但这并不容易。

In 2008 I was working about eighty hours a week doing my two full-time jobs (overseeing our investments and overseeing the company), and in my opinion not doing well enough at either. I felt that I, and the company more broadly, were slipping from being pervasively excellent. From the get-go I had toggled acceptably between investment management and business management. But now that we were a bigger company, the business management side was demanding much more time than I had to give it. I conducted a time-and-motion study of all of my investment and management responsibilities; it showed it would take me about 165 hours a week to achieve the level of excellence that I would be satisfied with in overseeing both our investments and management. That was obviously impossible. Since I wanted to delegate as much as possible, I asked whether the things I was doing could be done excellently by others, and if so, who those others were. Everyone agreed that most of those areas couldn’t adequately be delegated. I clearly hadn’t done a good enough job of finding and training others to whom I could delegate my responsibilities.
2008 年，我每周工作大约 80 小时，做我的两份全职工作（监督我们的投资和监督公司），在我看来，这两项工作都做得不够好。我觉得我，以及更广泛的公司，正在从普遍的优秀中滑落。从一开始，我就在投资管理和商业管理之间来回切换。但现在我们是一家更大的公司，业务管理方面需要的时间比我必须付出的时间要多得多。我对我的所有投资和管理职责进行了时间和运动研究;它表明，我每周需要大约 165 小时才能达到我在监督投资和管理方面感到满意的卓越水平。这显然是不可能的。因为我想尽可能地委托，所以我问我正在做的事情是否可以被其他人出色地完成，如果可以，其他人是谁。每个人都同意，这些领域中的大多数都不能得到充分的授权。显然，我在寻找和培训可以委派我的责任的其他人方面做得不够好。

To me, the greatest success you can have as the person in charge is to orchestrate others to do things well without you. A step below that is doing things well yourself, and worst of all is doing things poorly yourself. As I reflected on my position, I could see that despite all of my and Bridgewater’s amazing achievements, I had not achieved this highest level of success. In fact, I was still struggling to achieve the second-highest level (doing things well myself), even though Bridgewater was extremely successful.
对我来说，作为负责人，你能取得的最大成功是协调其他人在没有你的情况下把事情做好。再往下一步是自己把事情做好，最糟糕的是你自己把事情做得不好。当我反思自己的立场时，我可以看到，尽管我和 Bridgewater 取得了所有惊人的成就，但我并没有取得如此高水平的成功。事实上，我仍在努力达到第二高的水平（自己把事情做好），尽管布里奇沃特非常成功。

At the time, there were 738 people working at Bridgewater, with fourteen department heads. I oversaw the department heads, along with a Management Committee I’d created because I knew I couldn’t trust myself to know what was best without others probing me. I had structured the reporting lines so that I both reported to the Management Committee and held its members accountable for their oversight of the company. I wanted them to also own the responsibility of producing pervasive excellence and I wanted to be at their service in helping them achieve it.
当时，Bridgewater 有 738 名员工，有 14 个部门负责人。我监督了各部门负责人，以及我创建的管理委员会，因为我知道我不能相信自己在没有其他人探究的情况下知道什么是最好的。我构建了汇报关系，以便我既向管理委员会报告，又让其成员对他们对公司的监督负责。我希望他们也有责任创造无处不在的卓越，我想为他们服务，帮助他们实现这一目标。

In May 2008, I wrote an email to the five members of the Management Committee, copying the company, telling them that “I am escalating to let you know that I have reached my limits and that the quality of my work, and my work-life balance, are both suffering unacceptably.”
2008 年 5 月，我给管理委员会的 5 名成员写了一封电子邮件，抄送公司，告诉他们“我正在升级，让你们知道我已经达到了极限，我的工作质量和我的工作与生活的平衡都受到了不可接受的影响。

THE FINANCIAL AND ECONOMIC CRISIS OF 2008
2008 年的金融和经济危机

Recognizing that I was stretched wouldn’t by itself be enough to slow the flow of things coming at me, especially in the investment area at what proved to be a time of historic turbulence.
认识到我自己捉襟见肘并不足以减缓向我袭来的事情，尤其是在被证明是历史性动荡时期的投资领域。

Because too often I had been painfully surprised by different types of events that hadn’t happened to me before but happened in other times or other places—like the currency devaluation of 1971, or the debt crisis in the early 1980s—I’d developed our economic and market principles to be timeless and universal. In other words, I knew that we needed to understand all important economic and market movements, not just those that happened to me, and to make sure the principles we were using to position ourselves would have worked in all past times and all other countries.
因为我经常对以前从未发生在我身上但在其他时间或其他地方发生的不同类型的事件感到痛苦的惊讶——比如 1971 年的货币贬值，或者 1980 年代初的债务危机——我把我们的经济和市场原则发展成永恒的和普遍的。换句话说，我知道我们需要了解所有重要的经济和市场动向，而不仅仅是发生在我身上的那些，并确保我们用来定位自己的原则在过去所有时代和所有其他国家都适用。

As a result, back in the early 2000s, we had included a “depression gauge” in our systems that specified the actions we should take if a certain configuration of events began to play out in a way indicating a heightened risk of a debt crisis and depression. In 2007, this gauge indicated that a bubble of debt was nearing its bursting point because the costs of debt service were outpacing projected cash flows. Because interest rates were so close to 0 percent, I knew that central banks could not ease monetary policy enough to reverse the downturn the way they had in prior recessions. This was the exact configuration that had led to past depressions.
因此，早在 2000 年代初，我们在系统中包含一个“萧条量表”，它指定了如果某种类型的事件开始以表明债务危机和萧条风险增加的方式发展，我们应该采取的行动。2007 年，该指标表明债务泡沫接近破裂点，因为偿债成本超过了预期的现金流。由于利率如此接近 0%，我知道各国央行无法像之前的经济衰退那样放松货币政策，以扭转经济衰退。正是这种配置导致了过去的萧条。

My mind and gut flashed back to my 1979–82 experience. I was now both thirty years more knowledgeable and a whole lot less confident. While the dynamic in the economy seemed clear to me, I was much less sure I was right. I remembered how clearly it had seemed to me that the debt bust I’d been expecting in 1982 would sink the economy—and how painfully wrong I had turned out to be.
我的思想和直觉闪回了我 1979-82 年的经历。我现在的知识增加了 30 岁，但又大大减少了自信。虽然经济动态对我来说似乎很清楚，但我不太确定自己是对的。我清楚地记得，我一直期待的 1982 年债务萧条将使经济陷入沉沦——以及我被证明是多么痛苦的错误。

That experience also drove me to learn a lot more about debt crises and their effects on the markets, and I researched and traded through a number of them, including the Latin American debt crisis in the 1980s, the Japanese debt crisis of the 1990s, the blowup of Long-Term Capital Management in 1998, the bursting of the dot-com bubble in 2000, and the fallout from the attacks on the World Trade Center and Pentagon in 2001. With the help of my teammates at Bridgewater, I took history books and old newspapers and went day by day through the Great Depression and the Weimar Republic, comparing what happened then with what was happening in the present. The exercise only confirmed my worst fears: It seemed inevitable to me that large numbers of individuals, companies, and banks were about to have serious debt problems and that the Federal Reserve couldn’t lower interest rates to cushion the blow, as was the case in 1930–32.
那次经历也促使我对债务危机及其对市场的影响有了更多的了解，我研究了其中的一些危机并进行了交易，包括 1980 年代的拉丁美洲债务危机、1990 年代的日本债务危机、1998 年长期资本管理的爆炸、2000 年互联网泡沫的破灭。 以及 2001 年对世贸中心和五角大楼的袭击的后果。在布里奇沃特的队友的帮助下，我拿着历史书和旧报纸，日复一日地走过大萧条和魏玛共和国，将当时发生的事情与现在发生的事情进行比较。这次演习只是证实了我最担心的事情：在我看来，大量个人、公司和银行即将出现严重的债务问题似乎是不可避免的，而美联储无法像 1930-32 年那样降低利率来缓冲打击。

My fear of being wrong pushed me to seek out other smart folks to poke holes in my view. I also wanted to walk key policymakers through my thinking, both to stress-test it and to make them aware of the situation as I saw it, so I went to Washington to speak with people in both the U.S. Treasury and the White House. Though they were polite, what I was presenting seemed too far-fetched to them, especially when by all outward indications the economy seemed to be booming. Most of them didn’t go very deep into our reasoning or calculations before they dismissed them, with one exception: Ramsen Betfarhad, Vice President Dick Cheney’s deputy assistant of domestic policy. He worked through all our numbers and was concerned by them.
我对出错的恐惧促使我寻找其他聪明人来戳我的观点。我还想让主要政策制定者了解我的想法，既要对其进行压力测试，又要让他们了解我所看到的情况，所以我去了华盛顿，与美国财政部和白宫的人进行了交谈。尽管他们很有礼貌，但我所介绍的内容对他们来说似乎太牵强了，尤其是当所有外在迹象都表明经济似乎正在蓬勃发展时。他们中的大多数人在驳斥这些建议之前并没有深入研究我们的推理或计算，只有一个例外：副总统迪克·切尼（Dick Cheney）的国内政策副助理拉姆森·贝特法哈德（Ramsen Betfarhad）。他研究了我们所有的数字，并受到他们的关注。

Because everything we saw lined up and we couldn’t find anyone who could refute our views, we prepared our clients’ portfolios by balancing our positions in a way that there would be considerable upside and limited downside in the portfolios if we were right and putting in a backup plan in case we were wrong. Though we thought we were well prepared, we were as worried about being right as we were about being wrong. The prospect of the world economy going over a waterfall was scary to all of us because of what it might mean to those who weren’t protected.
因为我们看到的一切都是一致的，而且我们找不到任何可以反驳我们观点的人，所以我们通过平衡我们的头寸来准备客户的投资组合，如果我们是对的，投资组合将有相当大的上行空间和有限的下行空间，并制定备用计划以防我们错了。虽然我们认为我们已经做好了充分的准备，但我们对正确和错误一样担心。世界经济越过瀑布的前景对我们所有人来说都是可怕的，因为这对那些没有受到保护的人来说可能意味着什么。

As in 1982, when conditions deteriorated and circumstances increasingly transpired as we’d predicted, policymakers began to pay more attention to us. Betfarhad had me come to the White House to meet with him. Tim Geithner, president of the New York Fed, asked to see me as well. I brought Bob, Greg, and a young analyst named Bob Elliott to a lunch meeting with Geithner. We walked him through the numbers and he literally turned white. When he asked me where we’d gotten them from, I told him they were publicly available. We’d just put them together and looked at them in a different way.
就像 1982 年一样，当情况恶化，情况越来越多地发生正如我们预测的那样，政策制定者开始更加关注我们。贝特法哈德让我来白宫见他。纽约联储（New York Fed）行长蒂姆·盖特纳（Tim Geithner）也要求见我。我带 Bob、Greg 和一位名叫 Bob Elliott 的年轻分析师与 Geithner 共进午餐。我们向他介绍了数字，他真的脸色发白。当他问我从哪里得到它们时，我告诉他它们是公开的。我们只是把它们放在一起，以不同的方式看待它们。

Two days after our meeting with Geithner, Bear Stearns collapsed. That didn’t trigger much worry for most people or for the markets, though it was a sign of what was to come. It wasn’t until six months later in September, when Lehman Brothers collapsed, that everyone else connected the dots. At that point the dominoes fell fast, and though they couldn’t contain all the damage, policymakers, most importantly Fed chairman Ben Bernanke, reacted brilliantly to create “a beautiful deleveraging” (i.e., a way of lowering debt burdens while keeping economic growth positive and inflation low).8
在我们与 Geithner 会面两天后，贝尔斯登倒闭了。这并没有给大多数人或市场带来太多担忧，尽管这是即将发生的事情的征兆。直到六个月后的 9 月，雷曼兄弟 （Lehman Brothers） 倒闭，其他人才把这些点连上来。在那一刻，多米诺骨牌迅速倒下，尽管它们无法遏制所有的损害，但政策制定者，最重要的是美联储主席本·伯南克（Ben Bernanke）做出了出色的反应，创造了“一个美丽的去杠杆化”（即，一种在保持正经济增长和低通胀的同时降低债务负担的方法）。8

To make this long story short, we navigated this period well for our clients, anticipating market moves and avoiding losses. Our flagship fund made over 14 percent in 2008, a year when many other investors recorded losses of more than 30 percent. We would have done even better had we not feared being wrong, which led us to balance our bets instead of arrogantly and foolishly putting more chips at stake. But I had no regrets because I had learned that it wasn’t smart to bet that way. While in this case we would have made more money if we were less balanced, we certainly wouldn’t have survived and succeeded long enough to be in such a position if we’d approached our investments in that way.
长话短说，我们为客户很好地度过了这段时间，预测市场走势并避免了损失。我们的旗舰基金在 2008 年赚了 14% 以上，而许多其他投资者在这一年录得超过 30% 的亏损。如果我们不害怕犯错，我们会做得更好，这导致我们平衡我们的赌注，而不是傲慢和愚蠢地投入更多的筹码。但我并不后悔，因为我知道以这种方式下注并不明智。虽然在这种情况下，如果我们不那么平衡，我们会赚更多的钱，但如果我们以这种方式进行投资，我们肯定不会生存和成功足够长的时间，从而处于这样的位置。

The 2008 debt crisis was another one of those like the one in 1982, which were both like many more before them and many more that will come. I enjoyed reflecting back on my painful mistakes and the value of the principles they gave me. When the next big one comes along in twenty-five years or so, or who knows when, it will probably come as a surprise and cause a lot of pain unless those principles are properly encoded in algorithms put into our computers.
2008 年的债务危机是另一场与 1982 年类似的危机，这两者都是之前和将来的更多危机。我喜欢回顾我痛苦的错误以及他们给我的原则的价值。当下一个大事件在 25 年左右出现时，或者谁知道什么时候出现，除非这些原则被正确编码到我们计算机的算法中，否则它可能会让人感到惊讶并造成很多痛苦。

HELPING POLICYMAKERS
帮助政策制定者

Our economic and market principles were very different from most others, which accounted for our different results. I will explain these differences in Economic and Investment Principles and won’t digress into them now.
我们的经济和市场原则与大多数其他原则大不相同，这导致了我们不同的结果。我将在经济原则和投资原则中解释这些差异，现在不会岔开话题。

As former Fed chairman Alan Greenspan put it, “The models failed at a time when we needed them most . . . JP Morgan had the American economy accelerating three days before [the Lehman Brothers’ collapse]—their model failed. The Fed model failed. The IMF model failed . . . So that left me asking myself: What happened?” Bill Dudley, president of the New York Fed, homed in on the problem when he said, “I think there’s a fundamental problem in terms of how macroeconomists look at the economic outlook, growth, and inflation . . . If you look at the big macro models, they don’t have a financial sector typically in them. They don’t admit the possibility that the financial sector could essentially melt down, and therefore the monetary policy impulse could be completely impaired. So I think the lesson of the crisis is to do a lot more work to make sure that the finance people are talking to the macroeconomist people and building models that are more robust.” He was right. We “finance people” see the world very differently from the way economists do. As a result of our success, policymakers reached out to us more, which led me to have a lot more contact with senior economic policymakers in the U.S. and around the world. Out of respect for the privacy of our conversations, I won’t say much about them except to note that they became much more open to our nontraditional ways of looking at economies and markets, and more skeptical about traditional economic thinking, which had failed to either signal or avert the crisis.
正如前美联储主席艾伦·格林斯潘（Alan Greenspan）所说，“这些模型在我们最需要它们的时候失败了......摩根大通在 [雷曼兄弟倒闭] 前三天让美国经济加速——他们的模式失败了。美联储模式失败了。IMF 模型失败了......所以这让我问自己：发生了什么？纽约联邦储备银行（New York Fed）主席比尔·达德利（Bill Dudley）直面这个问题，他说：“我认为，就宏观经济学家如何看待经济前景、增长和通货膨胀而言，存在一个根本性的问题......如果你看一下大型宏模型，它们通常没有金融部门。他们不承认金融部门可能从根本上崩溃的可能性，因此货币政策冲动可能会完全受损。所以我认为这场危机的教训是做更多的工作，以确保财务人员与宏观经济学家交谈，并建立更稳健的模型。他是对的。我们“金融人”看待世界的方式与经济学家截然不同。由于我们的成功，政策制定者更多地与我们接触，这使我与美国和世界各地的高级经济决策者有了更多的联系。出于对我们谈话隐私的尊重，我不会对他们多说什么，只是要指出，他们对我们看待经济和市场的非传统方式变得更加开放，并且对传统经济思维更加怀疑，因为传统经济思维既没有发出信号，也没有避免危机。

Most of our exchanges were one-sided; I generally answered their questions and didn’t ask any that would put them in the awkward position of having to avoid answering for fear of compromising confidential information. I met with these leaders without making judgments and without regard for their particular ideologies. I approached them like a doctor, just wanting to make the most beneficial impact.
我们的大部分交流都是单方面的;我通常回答他们的问题，没有问任何会让他们处于尴尬境地的问题，因为害怕泄露机密信息而不得不避免回答。我与这些领导人会面时没有做出评判，也没有考虑他们的特定意识形态。我像医生一样接近他们，只是想产生最有益的影响。

They wanted my help because my global macroeconomic perspective as an investor was very different from theirs as policymakers. We were both products of our environments. Investors think independently, anticipate things that haven’t happened yet, and put real money at stake with their bets. Policymakers come from environments that nurture consensus, not dissent, that train them to react to things that have already occurred, and that prepare them for negotiations, not placing bets. Because they don’t benefit from the constant feedback about the quality of their decisions that investors get, it’s not clear who the good and bad decision makers among them are. They also have to be politicians. Even the most clear-sighted and capable policymakers must constantly divert their attention from the immediate problems they are dealing with to fight the objections of other policymakers, and the political systems they must navigate are often dysfunctional.
他们需要我的帮助，因为我作为投资者的全球宏观经济视角与他们作为政策制定者的观点截然不同。我们都是各自环境的产物。投资者独立思考，预测尚未发生的事情，并通过他们的赌注将真金白银置于赌注中。政策制定者来自培养共识而不是异议的环境，训练他们对已经发生的事情做出反应，并为谈判做好准备，而不是下注。因为他们没有从投资者获得的关于他们决策质量的持续反馈中受益，所以不清楚他们中的好决策者和坏决策者是谁。他们也必须是政治家。即使是最有远见、最有能力的政策制定者也必须不断将注意力从他们正在处理的紧迫问题上转移开来，以对抗其他政策制定者的反对意见，而他们必须驾驭的政治制度往往是功能失调的。

While the economic machine is more powerful than any political system in the long run (ineffective politicians will be replaced and incapable political systems will change), the interaction between the two is what drives economic cycles in the here and now—and it’s often not pretty to watch.
虽然从长远来看，经济机器比任何政治制度都更强大（无能的政客将被取代，无能的政治制度将被改变），但两者之间的互动是推动此时此地经济周期的动力——而且往往并不好看。

MAKING GREAT RETURNS
获得丰厚的回报

Our returns in 2010 were the best ever—nearly 45 and 28 percent in our two Pure Alpha funds and close to 18 percent in All Weather—almost exclusively because the systems we had programmed to take in information and process it were doing it superbly. These systems worked far better than we could with just our brains. Without them, we would have had to manage money the old and painful way: by trying to weigh in our heads all the markets and all the influences on them and then bring them together into a portfolio of bets. We would have had to hire and supervise a bunch of different investment managers, and because we couldn’t have blind faith in them, we’d have had to understand how each one made their decisions, which would mean watching what they were doing and why so we could know what to expect from them, while dealing with all their different personality issues. Why would I want to do that? It seemed to me that that way of investing or managing an organization was obsolete, like reading a map instead of following a GPS. Of course, building our system was hard work—it had taken us over thirty years to do it.
我们在 2010 年的回报率是有史以来最好的——我们的两只 Pure Alpha 基金分别接近 45% 和 28%，全天候基金接近 18%——这几乎完全是因为我们为接收和处理信息而编程的系统做得非常出色。这些系统比我们只用大脑工作得要好得多。没有它们，我们将不得不以古老而痛苦的方式管理资金：试图在我们的脑海中权衡所有市场及其所有影响，然后将它们整合成一个赌注组合。我们不得不雇用和监督一堆不同的投资经理，因为我们不能盲目地相信他们，所以我们必须了解每个人是如何做出决定的，这意味着要观察他们在做什么以及为什么，这样我们才能知道对他们有什么期望，同时处理他们所有不同的性格问题。我为什么要这样做？在我看来，这种投资或管理组织的方式已经过时了，就像阅读地图而不是遵循 GPS 一样。当然，构建我们的系统是一项艰巨的工作 — 我们花了 30 多年时间才完成。

Having too much money to manage can hurt performance, since the costs of getting in and out of positions can be high because being too big can push the markets. Making over 40 percent in 2010 had put us in the position of having to return a lot of money to clients who actually wanted to give us more to manage. We were always careful to stay safely short of being too big, lest we kill the goose that lays the golden eggs.
管理太多资金会损害业绩，因为进出头寸的成本可能很高，因为太大会推动市场。2010 年超过 40% 的收益使我们不得不将大量资金返还给真正想让我们管理更多资金的客户。我们总是小心翼翼地保持安全，以免弄死下金蛋的鹅。

Our clients didn’t want their money back—they wanted us to grow it. So we were presented with the puzzle of how to maximize our capacity without hurting our performance. We hadn’t looked at that before, because we’d never had that much money. We quickly discovered that if we just tweaked what we did and created a new fund that managed money the same way as Pure Alpha but invested it solely in the most liquid markets, our expected returns would be the same and the expected risk (i.e., volatility) only slightly higher.
我们的客户不希望拿回他们的钱 — 他们希望我们增加这笔钱。因此，我们面临着如何在不影响性能的情况下最大化我们的能力的难题。我们以前没有看过这个，因为我们从来没有那么多钱。我们很快发现，如果我们调整我们所做的，并创建一个新基金，以与 Pure Alpha 相同的方式管理资金，但仅将其投资于流动性最强的市场，我们的预期回报将是相同的，预期风险（即波动性）只会略高。

We programmed this new approach into our computers, back-tested it to see how it worked in all countries and time frames, and explained it to our clients in detail so they could thoroughly understand the logic behind it. As much as I love and have benefited from artificial intelligence, I believe that only people can discover such things and then program computers to do them. That’s why I believe that the right people, working with each other and with computers, are the key to success.
我们将这种新方法编程到我们的计算机中，对其进行回溯测试，以了解它在所有国家和时间框架内的运作方式，并向我们的客户详细解释，以便他们能够彻底理解其背后的逻辑。尽管我热爱人工智能并从中受益，但我相信只有人类才能发现这些事情，然后对计算机进行编程来完成它们。这就是为什么我相信合适的人，彼此合作并与计算机合作，是成功的关键。

Toward the end of the year, we opened “Pure Alpha Major Markets” and clients invested $15 billion in it. Since then its returns have been as expected—that is, about the same as Pure Alpha’s (actually better, but only slightly). Our clients were delighted. In fact, this new option was so popular that by 2011 we had to close it to new investment too.
临近年底，我们开设了“Pure Alpha Major Markets”，客户向其中投资了 150 亿美元。从那时起，它的回报就如预期的那样——也就是说，与 Pure Alpha 的差不多（实际上更好，但只是略有好）。我们的客户很高兴。事实上，这个新选项非常受欢迎，以至于到 2011 年，我们也不得不关闭它，接受新的投资。

GOING FROM BELOW THE RADAR TO ABOVE IT
从雷达之下到雷达之上

Success is a double-edged sword—as I learned after we anticipated the financial crisis and Bridgewater and I began to receive unwanted public attention. Our unusual performance, our unusual way of looking at economics and markets, and our unusual culture made us a continuing subject of interest. I wanted to stay under the radar so I avoided interacting with the press. That didn’t stop the press from writing about me and Bridgewater, which they typically did in a sensationalistic way—either painting me as a superhero investor who walked on water or as a leader of a cult, and sometimes both.
成功是一把双刃剑——正如我们在预见到金融危机以及我和布里奇沃特开始受到不必要的公众关注后所学到的那样。我们不同寻常的表现，我们看待经济和市场的不同寻常的方式，以及我们不同寻常的文化，使我们成为持续关注的主题。我想保持低调，所以我避免与媒体互动。这并没有阻止媒体报道我和布里奇沃特，他们通常以耸人听闻的方式进行报道——要么把我描绘成一个在水面上行走的超级英雄投资者，要么把我描绘成一个邪教的领袖，有时两者兼而有之。

Getting a lot of attention for being successful is a bad position to be in. Australians call it the “tall poppy syndrome,” because the tallest poppies in a field are the ones most likely to have their heads whacked off. I didn’t like the attention and I especially didn’t like the mischaracterizations of Bridgewater as a cult, because I felt it was hurting our ability to recruit great people. At the same time, I realized that because we didn’t let the media see how we truly operated inside Bridgewater, those sensational portrayals were unavoidable.
因为成功而获得很多关注是一个糟糕的位置。澳大利亚人称其为“高罂粟综合症”，因为田野里最高的罂粟花最有可能被砍掉头。我不喜欢这种关注，我特别不喜欢将布里奇沃特教会错误地描述为邪教，因为我觉得这损害了我们招募优秀人才的能力。与此同时，我意识到，因为我们没有让媒体看到我们在 Bridgewater 内部的真实运作方式，所以那些耸人听闻的描绘是不可避免的。

So I decided in late 2010 to make public my Principles—which explained exactly what we were doing and why. I put it on our website so it could be read freely and understood by people outside the company.
因此，我在 2010 年底决定公开我的原则——它准确地解释了我们正在做什么以及为什么。我把它放在我们的网站上，这样公司外部的人就可以自由阅读和理解它。

Doing that was a hard decision, but it turned out to be a great one. Most people got it and many beyond Bridgewater benefited from reading them. More than three million people have downloaded Principles; some even had it translated into their own languages at their own expense. I’ve received a large number of thank-you notes from people who said that reading Principles had changed their lives.
这样做是一个艰难的决定，但事实证明这是一个伟大的决定。大多数人都明白了，布里奇沃特以外的许多人都从阅读中受益。超过 300 万人下载了 Principles;有些人甚至自费将其翻译成自己的语言。我收到了大量来自人们的感谢信，他们说阅读 Principles 改变了他们的生活。

PREPARING BRIDGEWATER TO SUCCEED WITHOUT ME
让 BRIDGEWATER 在没有我的情况下取得成功做好准备

Since I was a kid, I’ve learned by doing. I’d just dive in after things I wanted and try to survive long enough to learn from my mistakes and improve. If I changed fast enough to become sustainable at whatever I was doing, then I would build on that to flourish. I’ve always had great faith in my ability to figure things out, and over time my need to figure things out made me better at doing so. As a result, I tended to hire people who were the same way—who would dive right into challenges, figure out what to do about them, and then do it. I figured that if they had great character, common sense, and creativity, and were driven to achieve our shared mission, they would discover what it took to be successful if I gave them the freedom to figure out how to make the right decisions. I knew that micromanaging and handcuffing them wouldn’t work because neither of us would like it. If I was the one telling them what to do, I wouldn’t be getting any leverage from them. Besides, I didn’t want to work with people who needed that.
从我还是个孩子的时候起，我就边做边学。我只是专注于我想要的事情，并尝试生存足够长的时间，以便从错误中吸取教训并取得进步。如果我的改变足够快，能够在我所做的任何事情上变得可持续，那么我就会在此基础上蓬勃发展。我一直对自己解决问题的能力充满信心，随着时间的推移，我对解决问题的需求使我更擅长这样做。因此，我倾向于雇用同样的人——他们会直接投入挑战，弄清楚如何应对这些挑战，然后去做。我认为，如果他们有良好的品格、常识和创造力，并致力于实现我们的共同使命，如果我给他们自由，让他们弄清楚如何做出正确的决定，他们就会发现成功需要什么。我知道微观管理和给他们戴上手铐是行不通的，因为我们都不喜欢这样。如果我是告诉他们该怎么做的人，我就不会从他们那里得到任何筹码。此外，我不想与需要的人合作。

But starting in the 1990s, I began to recognize the emotional barriers most people had to looking at their problems and weaknesses forthrightly. Rather than embracing ambiguous situations and difficult challenges, they tended to get uncomfortable when facing them. It is the rare bird who has the right mix of common sense, creativity, and character to shape change. Almost everyone needs help before they can get there. So I wrote down my principles and the logic behind them and shared them, hoping they could be used by those who thought they were good and debated openly by those who didn’t. I figured that over time we would all get in sync about how particular situations should be handled.
但从 1990 年代开始，我开始认识到大多数人在直截了当地看待自己的问题和弱点时所面临的情感障碍。他们没有接受模棱两可的情况和困难的挑战，而是在面对它们时往往会感到不舒服。它是一只稀有的鸟，它拥有常识、创造力和性格的正确组合来塑造变革。几乎每个人都需要帮助才能到达那里。所以我把自己的原则和背后的逻辑写下来分享，希望能被那些认为自己好的人用，被那些不认为好的人公开辩论。我想，随着时间的推移，我们都会在如何处理特定情况方面保持同步。

But while almost all of us quickly agreed on the principles intellectually, many still struggled to convert what they had agreed to intellectually into effective action. This was because their habits and emotional barriers remained stronger than their reasoning. The training and the virtual-reality tapes helped a lot, but they still weren’t enough.
但是，尽管我们几乎所有人都很快在理智上就这些原则达成了一致，但许多人仍然努力将他们在理智上达成一致的东西转化为有效的行动。这是因为他们的习惯和情感障碍仍然比他们的推理更强大。培训和虚拟现实录像带帮了大忙，但仍然不够。

No matter how much effort we put into screening new hires and training them to work in our idea meritocracy, it was inevitable that many of them would fall short. My approach was to hire, train, test, and then fire or promote quickly, so that we could rapidly identify the excellent hires and get rid of the ordinary ones, repeating the process again and again until the percentage of those who were truly great was high enough to meet our needs.
无论我们花了多少精力来筛选新员工并培训他们在我们的理念精英管理中工作，他们中的许多人都不可避免地会达不到要求。我的方法是快速招聘、培训、测试，然后解雇或晋升，这样我们就可以快速识别优秀员工并淘汰普通员工，一次又一次地重复这个过程，直到真正优秀的员工比例足够高，足以满足我们的需求。

But for this to work, we needed people with high standards who wouldn’t hesitate to eliminate people who couldn’t cut it. Many new employees (and some older ones) still were reluctant to probe hard at what people were like, which made things worse. It’s tough to be tough on people.
但要做到这一点，我们需要具有高标准的人，他们会毫不犹豫地淘汰那些无法削减的人。许多新员工（和一些老员工）仍然不愿意认真探究人们是什么样的，这让事情变得更糟。对人强硬是很难的。

Of course, most of the people who come to Bridgewater are adventurous types; they know what they’re getting into. They understand that the chances their job will not work out are higher than normal, but they embrace the risk because the upside of succeeding is huge relative to the downside of having it not work out. In the worst case they learn a lot about themselves, have an interesting experience, and leave for other jobs; in the best case, they become a part of an exceptional team achieving exceptional things.
当然，大多数来到布里奇沃特的人都是喜欢冒险的人。他们知道自己要做什么。他们明白他们的工作不成功的可能性比平时高，但他们接受风险，因为相对于不成功的好处，成功的好处是巨大的。在最坏的情况下，他们学到了很多关于自己的知识，获得了有趣的经历，并离开去做其他工作;在最好的情况下，他们成为卓越团队的一员，取得非凡的成就。

New hires typically go through an acclimation period of about eighteen to twenty-four months before becoming comfortable with the truthfulness and transparency that is such an essential part of the Bridgewater culture—especially accepting one’s mistakes and figuring out how to deal with them. But some people never adapt to it. I’ve been told that joining Bridgewater is a bit like joining an intellectual Navy SEALs; others describe it as going to a school of self-discovery run by someone like the Dalai Lama. The people who thrive say that while the period of adjustment is difficult, it is also joyous because of the excellence they achieve and the extraordinary relationships they make. And the ones who can’t or won’t adapt must be cut; this is essential to keeping Bridgewater excellent.
新员工通常会经历大约 18 到 24 个月的适应期，然后才能适应真实和透明，这是布里奇沃特文化的重要组成部分——尤其是接受自己的错误并弄清楚如何处理它们。但有些人从来没有适应它。有人告诉我，加入布里奇沃特有点像加入一个知识分子的海豹突击队;其他人则将其描述为去一所由达赖喇嘛这样的人开办的自我发现学校。茁壮成长的人说，虽然调整期很艰难，但也很快乐，因为他们取得了卓越的成就和建立的非凡关系。那些不能或不愿适应的人必须被裁掉;这对于保持 Bridgewater 的卓越表现至关重要。

For a long time, I had been the one responsible for establishing the culture and upholding its high standards. But in 2010, I was sixty years old and had been running Bridgewater for thirty-five years. Though I expected to be good for another ten years or so, I was ready to put my energy into other things. While I always wanted to be deep into the markets, I wanted to spend more time with my family and friends, to help policymakers, and to pursue a few growing passions (like ocean exploration and philanthropy) as well as whatever else interested me. My plan was to step out as CEO while helping my replacements as a mentor, remain in my investment role, and take the time I gained from no longer managing the company to suck the marrow out of life while I still could.
很长一段时间以来，我一直是负责建立这种文化并维护其高标准的人。但在 2010 年，我已经 60 岁了，已经经营 Bridgewater 35 年了。虽然我预计还会再好十年左右，但我已经准备好把精力投入到其他事情上。虽然我一直想深入市场，但我想花更多时间与家人和朋友在一起，帮助政策制定者，追求一些日益增长的激情（如海洋勘探和慈善事业）以及我感兴趣的任何其他事情。我的计划是退出 CEO 一职，同时作为导师帮助我的继任者，继续担任我的投资角色，并利用我不再管理公司所获得的时间，趁我还有机会的时候从生活中吸取骨髓。

As with all organizations, whether Bridgewater would succeed would come down to the people and the culture. People who run companies are faced with important choices every day. How they make those choices determines the character of the company, the quality of its relationships, and the outcomes it produces. When the buck stopped with me, I was responsible for most of the important decisions. Now those decisions would be in the hands of others. While they would have a well-established culture and agreed-upon principles that had worked for decades, the proof would be in the pudding.
与所有组织一样，Bridgewater 能否成功将归结为人和文化。经营公司的人每天都面临着重要的选择。他们如何做出这些选择决定了公司的性格、人际关系的质量以及它产生的结果。当责任停止在我身上时，我负责大多数重要的决定。现在，这些决定将掌握在其他人手中。虽然他们将拥有行之有效的成熟文化和已行之有效的商定原则，但证据就在布丁中。
